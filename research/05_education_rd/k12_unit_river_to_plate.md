# River → Plate: A 6-Week Interdisciplinary Unit for 8th Graders
## Grand Rapids CEA Agritech Hub — K-12 Education Program
**Designer:** Prof. Elena Cruz, Curriculum Developer, CEA Campus Design Council
**Grade Level:** 8th Grade (adaptable for Gr. 7–9)
**Duration:** 6 weeks (30 class periods of 50 minutes + 2 half-day field experiences)
**Design Framework:** Understanding by Design (UbD) — Backward Design
**Frameworks Applied:** Five S + TAG + Co-Star + CARE
**Date:** 2026

---

## STAGE 1 — DESIRED RESULTS

### Established Goals (NGSS Anchors)
| Code | Performance Expectation |
|---|---|
| **MS-LS2-1** | Analyze and interpret data to provide evidence for the effects of resource availability on organisms and populations of organisms in an ecosystem |
| **MS-LS2-3** | Develop a model to describe the cycling of matter and flow of energy among living and nonliving parts of an ecosystem |
| **MS-ESS3-3** | Apply scientific principles to design a method for monitoring and minimizing a human impact on the environment |
| **MS-ETS1-1** | Define the criteria and constraints of a design problem with sufficient precision to ensure a successful solution that takes into account relevant scientific principles and potential impacts on people and the natural environment |

### Michigan Science Standards Alignment
Michigan K-12 Science Standards are structurally identical to NGSS ([michigan.gov](https://www.michigan.gov/-/media/Project/Websites/mde/Literacy/Content-Standards/Science_Standards.pdf)); Michigan was an NGSS Lead State ([nextgenscience.org](https://www.nextgenscience.org/lead-state-michigan)). All four PEs above are fully applicable in Michigan.

### Common Core Connections
- **ELA:** CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-8.3 (follow multi-step technical procedures), CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.WHST.6-8.2 (write explanatory/informational texts), CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-8.7 (integrate quantitative data with text)
- **Math:** CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.A.2–3 (statistical distribution, center, spread), CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.7.RP.A.2 (proportional relationships — nutrient ratios, flow rates), CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.8.F.A.3 (linear functions — plant growth modeling)

### Understandings (Big Ideas)
Students will understand that:
1. Water connects river ecosystems to food production systems — what enters a river affects what ends up on our plates.
2. Controlled-environment agriculture can reduce human impacts on natural water systems while producing nutritious food year-round.
3. Engineers use criteria and constraints to design solutions that balance productivity, sustainability, and cost.
4. Pollinators are ecosystem engineers whose services underpin food security.

### Essential Questions
1. How does water travel from a river to a head of lettuce on your plate?
2. What tradeoffs does a farmer face when deciding how to grow food?
3. How can technology reduce the environmental footprint of agriculture?
4. Who are the non-human partners in food production, and what happens when they disappear?

### Knowledge — Students will know:
- Vocabulary: nutrient cycling, trophic level, closed-loop system, hydroponic, Electrical Conductivity (EC), pH, DLI (Daily Light Integral), IPM, pollinator, ecosystem services, runoff, watershed
- NGSS Science & Engineering Practices: Analyzing and interpreting data; Developing and using models; Constructing explanations; Designing solutions
- Crosscutting Concepts: Systems and system models; Cause and effect; Energy and matter (cycles); Stability and change

### Skills — Students will be able to:
- Read and interpret water quality data (pH, EC, turbidity) and graph trends
- Build and explain a nutrient cycle model for a hydroponic system
- Design an experiment with controlled variables (light, nutrient concentration)
- Write a structured engineering design brief with criteria and constraints
- Conduct a structured pollinator observation count using a transect protocol
- Present findings using data visualizations to a public audience

## STAGE 2 — ASSESSMENT EVIDENCE

### Summative Assessment: CEA Design Challenge (Weeks 5–6)
**Task:** Working in teams of 3–4, students design a small-scale CEA system (tabletop model or detailed blueprint) that could grow food for 100 school lunches per week using water from a captured/recycled source. The design must address:
1. Water source and treatment (nutrient solution setup)
2. Lighting plan (justify spectrum and daily light hours)
3. Pollinator integration strategy (how will crops be pollinated, or why not needed)
4. Environmental impact analysis (compare water use to field-grown equivalent)
5. A public presentation (5-minute pitch + Q&A to a panel including Hub staff)

### Summative Assessment Rubric

| Criterion | 4 — Exceeds | 3 — Meets | 2 — Approaching | 1 — Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Scientific Accuracy** (MS-LS2-1, MS-LS2-3) | All nutrient cycling and ecosystem claims are accurate; data cited from lab; no misconceptions | Most claims accurate; minor errors corrected with prompting | Several inaccuracies; some data used incorrectly | Significant errors; little evidence of scientific understanding |
| **Engineering Design** (MS-ETS1-1) | Criteria and constraints explicitly stated; design fully justified; tradeoffs analyzed | Criteria and constraints stated; design mostly justified | Criteria or constraints missing; design partially justified | No clear design rationale; constraints ignored |
| **Environmental Impact Analysis** (MS-ESS3-3) | Quantitative comparison (liters/kg) to field agriculture; reduction strategy explained with evidence | Comparison present; mostly quantitative | Comparison qualitative only; strategy vague | Impact not addressed |
| **Data Use** (MS-LS2-1) | Multiple datasets integrated; trends identified; statistics used correctly | Two datasets used; trends noted | One dataset used; interpretation incomplete | No data used |
| **Communication** | Clear, confident presentation; responds to questions with evidence | Mostly clear; responds to questions | Presentation unclear; struggles with questions | Difficult to follow; no responses to questions |
| **Teamwork / Equity of Contribution** | All members contribute equally; peer evaluation confirms | Most members contribute; minor imbalance | One member dominates | Clear free-riding; group dysfunction |

**Maximum Score: 24 points | Proficient: 16+ | Advanced: 21+**

## WEEK 1: River & Watershed (5 days)

### Driving Question
*Where does the water in your glass — and your lettuce — come from?*

### NGSS Focus: MS-ESS3-3

**Day 1 — Phenomenon Launch (50 min)**
- Hook: Show time-lapse of Grand River at flood stage vs. low water. Ask: "What changed? What caused it?"
- Introduce unit essential questions; students journal: "What do you already know about where food comes from?"
- Mini-lecture (15 min): Grand River watershed map; Grand Rapids in the watershed. What reaches the river from farms?
- Exit ticket: One thing that surprised you about the watershed.
*Materials:* Projector, Grand River watershed map (USGS), student science notebooks

**Day 2 — Water Quality Data Analysis (50 min)**
- Distribute Kent County water quality dataset (simplified, pre-downloaded from Kent County Drain Commissioner or teacher-created analog)
- Pairs read turbidity, nitrate, phosphorus data for 3 monitoring stations over 12 months
- Graph: line graph of nitrate levels by month; identify seasonal patterns
- Discussion: Why does nitrate spike in spring? (agricultural runoff connection)
- Debrief: How does farm fertilizer reach the river?
*Materials:* Water quality data worksheet, graph paper or Desmos access, pencils

**Day 3 — River Flow Math (50 min)**
*Math integration: CCSS 7.RP.A.2 — proportional reasoning*
- Mini-lesson: What is flow rate (L/min)? How do scientists measure it?
- Activity: Using mock data (cross-section width × depth × velocity), calculate volumetric flow for 3 river scenarios
- Challenge: If a CEA facility recirculates 95% of its water vs. field agriculture using 500 L/kg of produce, how much water does a 1,000 kg/year hydroponic lettuce operation save? (calculate, compare, discuss)
- Math journaling: students write two sentences explaining their calculation
*Materials:* Flow rate worksheet, calculators, student notebooks

**Day 4 — Watershed Model Build (50 min)**
- Groups of 4 build a mini-watershed model using foil pans, clay, and watered "soil" (sand/potting mix)
- Add "fertilizer" (baking soda colored with food coloring) upstream; observe runoff into "river"
- Record observations; identify where sediment and nutrients concentrate
- Connect to Grand River: urban storm drains carry what to the river?
*Materials:* Foil roasting pans, clay, potting mix, food coloring, watering cans, paper towels

**Day 5 — Synthesis & Week 1 Exit (50 min)**
- Gallery walk: groups post watershed model observations and 3 key learnings
- Class discussion: What design approaches could reduce agricultural runoff?
- Introduce CEA as one solution: show 30-second Hub promo video (or teacher-narrated photo tour)
- Week 1 exit ticket: 3-2-1 format
*Materials:* Sticky notes, chart paper, markers, Hub images/video

**Family Communication — Week 1:**
> *Dear Families: This week your student is beginning "River → Plate," a 6-week science unit connecting our local river ecosystem to the food we eat. Ask them tonight: "If fertilizer washes off a farm into the Grand River, what might happen to fish downstream?" We'll be doing math with real water-quality data, building watershed models, and eventually visiting the Grand Rapids CEA Agritech Hub. Permission forms for site visits will come home in Week 2.*

## WEEK 2: Nutrient Cycles & Hydroponic Chemistry (5 days)

### Driving Question
*How do plants get what they need to grow — without soil?*

### NGSS Focus: MS-LS2-3

**Day 1 — Matter Cycles Mini-Lecture (50 min)**
- Review: What do plants need? (light, water, CO₂, nutrients — N, P, K, micronutrients)
- Introduce nitrogen cycle: atmospheric N₂ → soil bacteria → nitrates → plant uptake → decomposition
- In hydroponics: nutrients dissolved directly in water — no soil bacteria step
- Annotated diagram: students draw and label the hydroponic nutrient cycle
*Materials:* Whiteboard/projector, blank diagram templates

**Day 2 — pH & EC Lab (50 min)**
- Safety briefing: pH meter and EC meter use; gloves and goggles on
- Activity: Students measure pH and EC of 4 solutions: tap water, nutrient solution A (low), nutrient solution B (target lettuce range), nutrient solution C (high)
- Record data in lab notebook; convert EC (mS/cm) to approximate nutrient concentration using provided conversion chart
- Discussion: What happens to lettuce if pH is too high? Too low?
*Materials:* pH meters (or pH strips), EC meters, 4 labeled beakers, safety goggles, gloves, lab notebooks

**Day 3 — Build a Hydroponic Model (50 min + continued Day 4)**
- Groups of 3 build a simple Kratky (passive) hydroponic unit: mason jar, net pot, hydroton clay pebbles, nutrient solution, lettuce seedling
- Label parts; photograph initial setup; record baseline EC, pH, plant height
- Begin data table that will be maintained through Week 4
*Materials:* Mason jars, net pots, hydroton, nutrient solution (pre-mixed by teacher), lettuce seedlings, rulers, pH strips, cameras/phones

**Day 4 — Light & Energy in CEA (50 min)**
*Science & Engineering Practice: Developing and Using Models*
- Mini-lesson: Daily Light Integral (DLI) — what it is, why it matters; brief introduction to photosynthesis equation
- Activity: Students calculate DLI for two scenarios (12 hrs × 200 μmol/m²/s vs. 16 hrs × 150 μmol/m²/s) using the formula: DLI = PPFD × photoperiod (h) × 0.0036
- Compare to natural DLI in Grand Rapids in January vs. July (teacher-provided data)
- Argument: Why does a CEA facility need artificial lighting in Michigan winter?
*Materials:* DLI calculation worksheet, calculators

**Day 5 — Nutrient Cycle Poster (50 min)**
*ELA integration: CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.WHST.6-8.2 — informational writing*
- Students create an annotated poster: "The Hydroponic Nutrient Cycle"
- Must include: matter inputs (water, nutrients, CO₂), plant uptake, outputs (O₂, plant biomass, depleted solution), recycling loop
- Beneath poster: one paragraph explaining how this cycle differs from a field-agriculture nutrient cycle
- Post and do a 15-minute gallery walk with sticky-note feedback from peers
*Materials:* Poster paper, markers, reference diagrams

**Family Communication — Week 2:**
> *Dear Families: This week your student is learning about nutrient cycles and hydroponics — growing plants in water without soil. They built their own small hydroponic unit and are measuring pH and electrical conductivity of the nutrient solution. Ask them to show you their data table. A signed permission slip for our site visit to the CEA Agritech Hub (Week 3) is enclosed. Please return by [date].*

## WEEK 3: CEA Observation Lab — Site Visit (2 days on-site + 3 prep/debrief days)

### Driving Question
*How do scientists and engineers collect data to make decisions in a real CEA facility?*

### NGSS Focus: MS-LS2-1 (data analysis and interpretation)

**Day 1 — Pre-Visit Preparation (50 min)**
- Introduce data collection protocols students will use on-site:
  - Yield measurement: count and weigh lettuce heads per tray
  - Growth rate estimation: measure 5 random plants per variety; photograph
  - Environmental readings: pH, EC, temperature, humidity (students use Hub instruments with supervision)
  - Observation sketches: draw 3 different crop varieties with labeled morphological features
- Assign team roles: Data Recorder, Measurer, Photographer, Sketcher (rotate)
- Practice: run through protocol with classroom plants
- Distribute observation packets (pre-printed data tables + observation prompts)
*Materials:* Observation packets, clipboards, pencils, rulers

**Day 2 — Site Visit: CEA Agritech Hub (Half Day, ~3.5 hours)**
- *Transportation: school or Hub-arranged bus; 1:10 adult:student supervision ratio*
- **Station 1 (45 min): Hydroponic grow floor** — students collect yield data, measure pH/EC, photograph growth stages
- **Station 2 (30 min): Energy systems overview** — Dr. Lena Watts or Hub engineer presents micro-hydro turbine; students ask prepared questions about energy balance
- **Station 3 (30 min): Apiary observation deck** — Amira Bloom or apiary staff leads safe observation; students count bee activity (see Week 4 protocol)
- **Station 4 (30 min): Post-harvest kitchen** — observe packaging; taste fresh CEA lettuce vs. grocery store sample (blind taste test data collected)
- **Debrief (15 min):** Students write 3 key observations and 1 remaining question in notebooks
*Materials:* Observation packets, pencils, rulers, permission slips, allergy list on file with Hub staff

**Day 3 — Data Entry & Graphing (50 min)**
- Students enter yield data into shared class spreadsheet (Google Sheets or printed)
- Each student creates one graph: yield (g/tray) by crop variety OR EC vs. growth rate (scatter plot)
- Identify outlier data points; discuss possible explanations
*Materials:* Computers/tablets or graph paper, data from site visit

**Day 4 — Claim-Evidence-Reasoning Writing (50 min)**
*ELA integration: CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.WHST.6-8.2*
- Mini-lesson: CER (Claim-Evidence-Reasoning) structure
- Prompt: "Write a CER paragraph: Does resource availability (nutrient concentration) affect lettuce yield at the CEA Hub? Use your data as evidence."
- Peer review using provided feedback sentence starters
*Materials:* CER graphic organizer, student data

**Day 5 — Ecosystem Model Revision (50 min)**
- Students revisit their Week 2 nutrient cycle posters; add new information from site visit
- Add: energy inputs (electricity from turbine + grid), outputs (harvested biomass, heat), and water recycling loop
- Exit ticket: "How is the CEA facility similar to and different from a natural ecosystem?"

**Family Communication — Week 3:**
> *Dear Families: This week your student visited the Grand Rapids CEA Agritech Hub! They collected real scientific data, observed pollinator activity, and tasted fresh lettuce grown without soil just miles from school. Ask them what surprised them most. Their observation data are being used this week to write their first scientific argument paragraph.*

## WEEK 4: Pollinators, Ecosystem Services & the Bee Lab (5 days)

### Driving Question
*What would happen to our food system if pollinators disappeared?*

### NGSS Focus: MS-LS2-1 (populations), MS-LS2-3 (matter cycles / ecosystem services)

**Day 1 — Pollinator Ecology (50 min)**
- Hook: Show data on global pollinator decline (honeybee colony losses, wild bee species reduction)
- Mini-lecture: What is an ecosystem service? Pollination as ecosystem service; economic value ($15B+ annually in U.S. crop pollination)
- Introduce Varroa destructor mite: primary driver of honeybee colony collapse; management strategies (oxalic acid, integrated mite management)
- Student task: annotate a diagram of a hive showing how Varroa infestation spreads through a colony
*Materials:* Pollinator decline data (USDA NASS / Bee Informed Partnership), hive diagram

**Day 2 — Pollinator Count Field Protocol (50 min, outdoors or via video)**
*Option A (spring/fall): Conduct outdoor transect count on school grounds or nearby green space*
*Option B (winter): Use standardized 5-minute video observation protocol with prerecorded transect video from Hub apiary*
- Protocol: 5-minute observation at a 1m × 1m "observation window" on a flowering plant; record species (bee, butterfly, fly, other), frequency of visits per minute, and plant species
- Groups of 3; rotate through 3 observation stations
- Compile class data; calculate mean visits/minute by pollinator type
*Materials:* Clipboards, tally sheets, field identification card (honeybee, bumblebee, monarch, syrphid fly), stopwatches/phones

**Day 3 — Data Analysis: Pollinator Counts (50 min)**
*Math integration: CCSS 6.SP.A — statistics and probability*
- Enter class pollinator count data into frequency table
- Calculate mean, median, and range for bee visits per minute
- Create a bar chart: species diversity at each observation station
- Discussion: Does species diversity differ by station? What environmental factors might explain this?
*Materials:* Data tables, graph paper or Desmos, calculators

**Day 4 — Honey Chemistry & Nutrition (50 min)**
- Mini-lab: compare Brix reading (sugar content) of Hub raw honey vs. commercial honey using hand refractometer
- Examine honey under magnifying glass; identify pollen grains if visible
- Reading: 1-page adapted text on colony health indicators (winter cluster size, brood pattern, mite counts)
- ELA connection: students annotate the text using a provided annotation guide (main idea, evidence, unfamiliar vocabulary)
*Materials:* Refractometers, honey samples (Hub raw + commercial), magnifying glasses, reading text

**Day 5 — Ecosystem Services Argumentation (50 min)**
*ELA integration: CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.WHST.6-8.1 — arguments from evidence*
- Prompt: "A CEA developer proposes eliminating the outdoor apiary to reduce costs. Write a scientific argument for or against this decision, citing evidence from your pollinator data and reading."
- Students write a 3-paragraph argument (claim, evidence × 2, counterargument + rebuttal)
- Pair-share: students exchange drafts, provide one "I notice / I wonder" comment
*Materials:* Lined paper or digital doc, data from Days 2–3, reading from Day 4

**Family Communication — Week 4:**
> *Dear Families: This week your student is studying pollinators — the bees, butterflies, and other insects that make much of our food possible. They conducted a pollinator count using real field science protocols and tasted raw honey from the CEA Hub's apiary. Ask them to explain the difference between a managed honeybee hive and a wild bumble bee nest. Next week we move from the field to the kitchen — and then to the design lab!*

## WEEK 5: Farm-to-Cafeteria Tasting & Design Brief Launch (5 days)

### Driving Question
*How do we design a food system that is good for people, pollinators, and the planet?*

### NGSS Focus: MS-ETS1-1 (define criteria and constraints)

**Day 1 — Farm-to-Cafeteria Tasting Event (50 min)**
- Hub delivers (or school cafeteria prepares) a simple salad made entirely from CEA-grown produce (lettuce, herbs, radish microgreens)
- Students complete a structured sensory evaluation card: appearance, aroma, texture, flavor (1–5 scale + descriptive words)
- Calculate class mean sensory scores; compare to Day 2/Week 3 grocery-store comparison data
- Discussion: What makes food "local"? What are the tradeoffs of local vs. globally sourced food?
*Materials:* Salad ingredients (Hub-sourced), plates, forks, sensory evaluation cards, calculators

**Day 2 — Design Challenge Introduction (50 min)**
- Reveal summative assessment task (see Stage 2 above)
- Mini-lecture: Engineering Design Process (Define → Research → Ideate → Prototype → Test → Iterate)
- Introduce criteria and constraints:
  - **Criteria (success metrics):** Must produce ≥100 servings/week; water use <50 L/kg produce; net zero fossil-fuel energy preferred
  - **Constraints (limitations):** Budget $50,000 capital; footprint ≤500 sq ft; must be operable by one part-time staff member; school/urban setting
- Teams of 3–4 form; assign roles (Lead Designer, Data Analyst, Presenter, Note-Taker)
*Materials:* Design challenge handout, engineering design process poster, team role cards

**Day 3 — Research Day (50 min)**
- Teams research one aspect of their design using provided resource binder (printed excerpts from MSU OptimIA research, Cornell GLASE materials, Freight Farms campus case studies)
- Guiding questions: What crop grows fastest under artificial light? What nutrient solution is recommended for lettuce? How much does a LED grow light cost to operate per year?
- Record 5+ cited facts in design notebook
*Materials:* Resource binder, design notebooks, pencils (no internet required; optional if computer access available)

**Day 4 — Ideation & Sketching (50 min)**
- Each team member sketches one possible system configuration (solo ideation, 10 min)
- Team presents sketches to each other; uses 2 stars + 1 wish feedback
- Teams select best elements from each sketch; create composite preliminary design
- Begin labeling: water source, nutrient delivery, lighting, pollinator strategy, harvest pathway
*Materials:* Blank design templates, rulers, pencils, colored pencils

**Day 5 — Design Brief Draft (50 min)**
*ELA integration: CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.WHST.6-8.2 — informational/explanatory writing*
- Teams write a formal Design Brief: one page with sections: Problem Statement, Criteria & Constraints, Proposed Solution (describe system), Evidence of Feasibility (cite 2+ sources), Environmental Impact (calculate water savings vs. field)
- Teacher circulates; provides written feedback on drafts
- Peer review using provided rubric row for "Environmental Impact Analysis"
*Materials:* Design brief template, rubric, pencils or word processor

**Family Communication — Week 5:**
> *Dear Families: This week your student tasted a salad grown entirely at the CEA Agritech Hub and launched their final engineering design challenge. Teams of 3–4 are designing a small food-growing system that could feed 100 school lunches per week using recycled water and clean energy. Their designs will be presented to a panel of experts next week. You are warmly invited to attend the presentations on [DATE, TIME, LOCATION]. Your student's design brief draft comes home this Friday — ask them to explain their biggest engineering challenge.*

## WEEK 6: Design Challenge — Prototype, Present & Reflect (5 days)

### Driving Question
*Does our design actually work? How do we know?*

### NGSS Focus: MS-ETS1-1 through ETS1-4

**Day 1 — Design Revision & Prototype Build (50 min)**
- Teams incorporate teacher and peer feedback; revise design brief
- Build physical prototype OR complete detailed technical blueprint (choose based on available materials)
- Prototype option: tabletop model using cardboard, foam, tape, small LED, mason jar, clear tubing
- Blueprint option: scaled drawing (1 cm = 1 ft) with labeled components and materials list
*Materials:* Cardboard, foam board, tape, scissors, small LED strips (teacher-sourced), mason jars, or rulers and graph paper for blueprints

**Day 2 — Test & Data Collection (50 min)**
- Teams attempt a "proof-of-concept" test: fill hydroponic component with nutrient solution; measure and record pH, EC; verify light placement
- Complete test data table: Did pH meet target range (5.5–6.5)? Did EC meet target (1.2–2.0 mS/cm for lettuce)? Does light reach growing surface?
- Document failures; iterate: if pH out of range, adjust and retest
- Record iteration log: what changed, why, what happened
*Materials:* pH strips or meters, EC meters, nutrient solution, test data table, iteration log

**Day 3 — Presentation Preparation (50 min)**
- Teams prepare a 5-minute presentation (visual aid required: poster, slide deck, or physical model)
- Practice run: full run-through timed; coach each other on clarity and evidence use
- Each presenter rehearses their section; team ensures every member speaks
- Teacher provides feedback on one thing to improve before presentations
*Materials:* Presentation materials, timer, rubric

**Day 4 — Public Presentations (full 50 min)**
- Panel: teacher + Hub staff member (invited) + one student from another class (peer evaluator)
- Each team presents 5 minutes; panel asks 2 questions; team responds
- Audience completes peer feedback forms
- Teacher uses summative rubric (Stage 2) for scoring
*Materials:* Rubric, feedback forms, timer, Hub staff invitation letter (teacher arranges in advance)

**Day 5 — Reflection & Unit Celebration (50 min)**
- Students complete individual reflection: "What is the most important thing you learned? What would you do differently? How does what you learned connect to your life?"
- Class creates a collaborative "River → Plate" data wall: post best graphs, posters, and photos from all 6 weeks
- Share harvest: if classroom hydroponic units have lettuce ready, harvest and taste as a class
- Distribute CEA Hub "Young Scientist" certificate (Hub-designed; teacher prints)
*Materials:* Reflection handout, student work to display, CEA Hub certificate template

**Family Communication — Week 6 (Invitation):**
> *Dear Families: We invite you to our "River → Plate" Design Showcase on [DATE] at [TIME] in [ROOM]. Your student's team will present their CEA food system design to a panel that includes staff from the Grand Rapids CEA Agritech Hub. Come hear what your scientist has been working on! Light refreshments featuring locally grown produce will be served.*

## Differentiation

**For students who need additional support:**
- Provide graphic organizers for CER writing (claim sentence starter, evidence template, reasoning frame)
- Pair with a Lab Partner who has stronger data literacy; assign complementary roles
- Allow oral presentation in lieu of written design brief
- Provide simplified data tables with units pre-labeled

**For students who need additional challenge:**
- Research and propose a second crop (e.g., tomatoes) requiring pollination — and design the pollination system
- Calculate full lifecycle water footprint including manufacturing of hydroponic equipment
- Write a letter to the school board proposing a school hydroponic program with cost-benefit analysis
- Create an infographic for the school website explaining the River → Plate journey

**English Language Learners:**
- Provide bilingual vocabulary cards (English/Spanish for Grand Rapids context; other languages as needed)
- Allow science notebooks in home language with English key terms
- Pre-teach vocabulary clusters before each week's activities using visual glossary
