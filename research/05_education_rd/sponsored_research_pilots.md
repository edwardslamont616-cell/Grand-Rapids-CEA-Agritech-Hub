## Overview

These five pilot research projects are scoped at $40,000–$80,000 each — a range designed to fit within a single industry or small-grant funding cycle while generating sufficient publishable data to anchor a follow-on USDA NIFA AFRI or SCRI competitive grant. Each pilot is designed to be led by a named or to-be-named (TBN) faculty member from MSU AgBioResearch, GVSU, or Michigan Tech, with Hub staff providing research infrastructure, data collection support, and extension outreach.

**Funding sources for pilots:** MDARD SCBGP ($125K max), USDA VAPG (planning component), MSU Project GREEEN (seed funding for MSU-led projects), GVSU Research Development grants, or Hub operating budget for highest-priority pilot.

## Pilot 1: Dynamic LED Spectrum × Leaf Lettuce Yield and Nutrient Density

### Scientific Rationale
MSU's OptimIA project demonstrated that far-red light increases lettuce leaf area and yield, while blue light enhances pigmentation and antioxidant content ([canr.msu.edu](https://www.canr.msu.edu/news/controlled-environment-agriculture-research-sees-innovation-through-project-greeen)). However, the optimal dynamic spectrum — changing the red:blue:far-red ratio throughout the growing cycle — has not been fully characterized at the commercial scale, particularly for Michigan winter conditions and the specific nutrient solution profiles used in hydroponic lettuce production. This pilot directly extends the OptimIA and CEA Lighting Lab work to a community-accessible, commercially operating facility.

### Research Questions
1. Does a dynamic LED spectrum protocol (blue-dominant in early growth, red:far-red balanced in late growth) increase fresh weight yield of butterhead lettuce compared to a static red:blue spectrum?
2. Do spectrum-treated plants show measurably different antioxidant content (ORAC value) and nitrate content at harvest?
3. What is the energy cost per kilogram of produce under each lighting treatment, and what is the economic breakeven point for premium-priced nutritionally enhanced lettuce?

### Methods (Overview)
- Design: 3 × 4 randomized complete block design; 3 spectrum treatments × 4 replications per treatment; 2 crop cycles (12 weeks each)
- Grow space: 3 × 100 sq ft dedicated research bays at Hub; Kratky or DWC with standardized nutrient solution
- Measurements: fresh weight (g/plant), canopy spread (cm), SPAD chlorophyll reading (weekly), ORAC assay at harvest (contracted to MSU food science lab), energy logger per bay
- Statistical analysis: ANOVA with Tukey HSD post-hoc; effect size reporting

### Proposed Faculty Lead
**Dr. Erik Runkle**, Professor, MSU Department of Horticulture / Project GREEEN PI ([canr.msu.edu](https://www.canr.msu.edu)) — or **Dr. Roberto Lopez**, Associate Professor, MSU Horticulture, as co-PI given his CEA HERB project expertise

## Pilot 2: Varroa Management in Indoor Bee Storage (Winter Cluster Under CEA Microclimate)

### Scientific Rationale
Varroa destructor mite is the primary driver of managed honeybee colony losses in Michigan and across North America. Most Varroa management research focuses on outdoor hive conditions. The CEA Hub's indoor apiary creates a unique opportunity to test whether controlled indoor wintering (stable temperature 8–12°C, low humidity) combined with a late-season oxalic acid treatment protocol produces lower spring Varroa levels and stronger colony buildup compared to outdoor wintering. This directly addresses a critical knowledge gap for northern CEA apiaries and supports the Hub's Beekeeping Technician Certificate with live research data.

### Research Questions
1. Does indoor wintering of honeybee colonies at controlled temperature (8–12°C) reduce Varroa infestation rates at spring inspection compared to outdoor wintering under Michigan conditions?
2. Does an integrated treatment protocol (oxalic acid vapor in December + trickling in February) combined with indoor conditions achieve infestation rates below the 2% economic threshold at April inspection?
3. What are the colony size (frames of bees) and honey reserve differences between indoor and outdoor wintered colonies at spring emergence?

### Methods (Overview)
- 8 colonies: 4 indoor (Hub climate-controlled space), 4 outdoor (Hub grounds)
- Varroa counts via alcohol wash (n=300 bees/hive) at October, December, February, April
- Colony assessments (frames of bees, honey frames, queen status) at September and April
- Oxalic acid vaporization protocol applied uniformly to all colonies in December and February
- Data: paired t-tests; effect size; mortality rates

### Proposed Faculty Lead
**To-Be-Named (TBN):** MSU Department of Entomology or GVSU Biology Department faculty with experience in apiculture/bee health research. Possible contact: MSU Extension Bee Health, or Michigan State Beekeepers Association for faculty referral.

## Pilot 3: Hydroponic Nutrient Regime × Nutrient Density in Specialty Microgreens

### Scientific Rationale
Microgreens (7–14 day harvested seedlings of brassicas, sunflower, peas, and other species) command premium retail prices ($25–$50/lb) and contain measurably higher concentrations of vitamins C, E, and K compared to mature crops. However, no published research has systematically compared the effect of varying N:P:K ratios, organic vs. mineral nutrient sources, and EC levels on the nutrient density outcomes of commercially grown microgreens in a hydroponic setting. This pilot fills that gap and directly supports Hub revenue goals by identifying the protocol that maximizes both yield and nutritional value for market positioning.

### Research Questions
1. Does varying the nitrogen source (ammonium vs. nitrate) or N:P:K ratio affect ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) and tocopherol (Vitamin E) content in sunflower and pea shoot microgreens?
2. What EC range (0.8–1.5 mS/cm vs. 1.5–2.5 mS/cm) optimizes fresh weight yield without sacrificing nutrient density?
3. Is there a measurable flavor or texture difference (via consumer panel) between low-N and high-N microgreen treatments?

### Methods (Overview)
- 6 nutrient treatments (3 EC × 2 N-source) × 3 crop species (sunflower, pea shoot, radish) × 3 replications per treatment
- 3 grow cycles (21 days each); continuous harvest
- Measurements: fresh weight (g/tray), nutrient analysis (MSU food science lab: Vitamin C, E by HPLC), consumer sensory panel (15 participants per treatment)
- Statistical: mixed-model ANOVA; treatment × species interaction

### Proposed Faculty Lead
**Dr. Simone Valle de Souza**, Assistant Professor, MSU Department of Horticulture (horticulture economics and indoor crop quality, OptimIA co-investigator) — or GVSU Biomedical Science TBN faculty for nutritional analysis component

## Pilot 4: Low-Cost Food Safety Sensor Networks for Small CEA Operations

### Scientific Rationale
The FDA Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) Produce Safety Rule imposes water testing, sanitation, and recordkeeping requirements on CEA operations. Large commercial operators can afford enterprise-level farm management systems ($10,000–$50,000/year). Operators at the scale of the Grand Rapids Hub (~10,000 sq ft) and similar small community CEA farms face significant compliance costs relative to revenue. This pilot tests whether a custom IoT sensor network — built from off-the-shelf components (Raspberry Pi, ESP32, open-source firmware) — can automate the data collection required for FSMA compliance at a capital cost under $2,000, and whether the resulting data quality meets FDA audit standards.

### Research Questions
1. Can a low-cost IoT sensor package (pH, EC, temperature, turbidity, dissolved oxygen, humidity) provide data of sufficient accuracy and traceability for FSMA produce safety compliance documentation?
2. Does automated sensor-based monitoring reduce the time burden of compliance recordkeeping for small CEA operators (survey-based measurement)?
3. What are the critical failure modes of low-cost sensors in a humid, chemically challenging CEA environment, and how can they be mitigated through maintenance protocol?

### Methods (Overview)
- Deploy 3 sensor node configurations (commercial baseline, mid-tier IoT, custom open-source) at Hub for 6 months
- Calibration protocol: weekly side-by-side comparison with certified laboratory instruments
- Operator time study: log time spent on manual recordkeeping under each configuration
- Failure log: document sensor drift, failure events, false alarms
- Produce safety audit simulation: Hub invites MDARD inspector for informal review of automated records

### Proposed Faculty Lead

## Pilot 5: Workforce Pipeline Outcomes — CEA Certificate Completers (6-Month Longitudinal Study)

### Scientific Rationale
There is a critical gap in the literature on workforce outcomes for short-term community college certificates in emerging sectors like CEA. While CEA industry groups point to a significant workforce shortage ([glase.org CEA Works](https://glase.org/resources/ceaworks/)), no longitudinal study has tracked employment placement, wage outcomes, and job satisfaction for certificate completers in a Midwest urban setting. This pilot produces that evidence base — which is prerequisite for future Going PRO Talent Fund applications, workforce development grant renewals, and policy advocacy for MDE CTE Agriculture program expansion.

### Research Questions
1. What percentage of CEA Operations Certificate completers are employed in a food/agriculture-adjacent job within 6 months of graduation?
2. What is the average wage change from pre-enrollment to 6-month post-graduation for participants who were previously employed in a different sector?
3. Do participants who accessed Michigan Reconnect funding report different outcomes from self-paying participants?
4. What barriers did non-completers encounter, and what program modifications would address them?

### Methods (Overview)
- Participants: 2 cohorts (40 students total) from GRCC CEA Operations Certificate
- Data collection: baseline survey at enrollment; exit survey at graduation; 6-month follow-up survey
- Employment verification: self-reported + consent to employment record verification
- Wage data: self-reported hourly wage or salary
- Analysis: descriptive statistics, pre/post comparison, subgroup analysis by funding source, second-chance status
- IRB: GVSU or GRCC IRB review required

### Proposed Faculty Lead
