## 1. Purpose and Scope

This safety plan governs all visitor and educational programming involving live honey bees at the CEA Campus Agritech Hub, including:
- Observation hive viewing (unsuited, behind glass)
- Suited apiary tours (outdoor apiary and rooftop)
- K-12 and higher-ed educational programs at the apiary
- Staff beekeeping operations
- Special events involving bees or bee products

This plan does not govern the bumble bee hives in the tomato greenhouse (those are restricted to staff access only and represent minimal sting risk during routine visitor observation through glass panels).

---

## 2. Hazard Identification

### 2.1 Primary Hazard: Bee Stings
Honey bee stings cause two categories of health response:

| Response Type | Affected Population | Signs | Management |
|--------------|-------------------|-------|------------|
| Local reaction (normal) | All individuals | Pain, redness, swelling at sting site | Ice, antihistamine; self-limiting |
| Large local reaction | ~10–15% of people | Swelling extending to whole limb; resolves in days | Antihistamine; medical follow-up recommended |
| Systemic allergic reaction (anaphylaxis) | ~1–3% of population | Hives, throat swelling, difficulty breathing, drop in blood pressure | **Medical emergency — EpiPen + 911 immediately** |

**Risk context:** Bees rarely sting unless physically disturbed, threatened, or defensive. Well-managed honey bee colonies in inspected hives are significantly less likely to sting than feral colonies. Proper PPE eliminates nearly all sting risk during suited tours.

### 2.2 Secondary Hazards
- Trip and fall in uneven apiary terrain
- Heat illness during summer suited tours (bee suits restrict cooling)
- Equipment-related injuries (hive tool, smoker burns)
- Sun exposure during outdoor activities

## 3. Observation Hive Specifications

### 3.1 Design Requirements
Per [University of Florida / IFAS observation hive guidelines (ENY131/MG320)](https://ask.ifas.ufl.edu/publication/MG320) and [NCSU observation hive setup guide](https://wncbees.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Setting-up-an-observation-hive-NCSU-2.15.pdf):

| Feature | Specification |
|---------|--------------|
| Frame capacity | Permanent display: 4–6 frames (larger is better for colony stability) |
| Glazing | Tempered glass or Plexiglass (regular window glass not recommended due to break risk) |
| Placement | Interior visitor center; glass panels visible from both sides |
| Bee entrance/exit | Sealed PVC tube through wall to exterior; entrance directed away from pedestrian traffic |
| Entrance closure | Sliding vertical tin insert or equivalent for easy closure during events or transport |
| Queen marking | Queen always marked with paint pen (current year color code) for easy location |
| Feed station | Perpetual feeder; monitored weekly; prevents resource stress |
| Stability | Fastened to floor or wall frame; cannot tip; beyond arm's reach of visitors |
| Buffer space | Minimum 24" clear zone around glass; display area for supplementary educational materials |
| Lighting | Red-spectrum LED inside the hive unit (bees are calm under red light); white/ambient room lighting OK outside glass |
| Backup colony | Second observation hive or dedicated source colony maintained ≥2 miles from campus for rapid replacement |

**Recommended vendor sources:** Knox Cellars, Brushy Mountain, Mann Lake — all offer permanent multi-frame observation hive units in the $300–$600 range.

### 3.2 Observation Hive Management Protocol
- **Weekly inspection:** Staff check queen presence, food stores, bee population, and overall colony health using red headlamp
- **Monthly deep inspection:** Open hive (inside closed observation unit) to check brood pattern and Varroa levels (using sticky board count as trend indicator only; conduct alcohol wash quarterly)
- **Colony replacement:** If colony becomes queenless, drops below 2 frames of bees, or shows disease signs, replace from source colony. Replace unit contents rather than letting it decline
- **Seasonal management:** Monitor for swarming impulse in spring (add frames if population outgrows unit; keep queen clipped as optional preventive)
- **Winter protocol:** Reduce entrance; supplement feed; observe cluster weekly

## 4. Suited Apiary Tour Protocol

### 4.1 Participant Eligibility
| Category | Guidance |
|----------|---------|
| Adults (18+) | Full participation with signed waiver |
| Youth (12–17) | Full participation; parent/guardian signed waiver required; parent must accompany |
| Children (under 12) | Observation hive viewing only; no suit tours without program-specific risk assessment |
| Individuals with known bee sting allergy | Not permitted on suited tours; observation hive viewing behind glass is safe alternative |
| Pregnant individuals | Permitted; advise to inform guide; no specific bee venom hazard identified in research, but standard caution applies |
| Maximum group size | **20 participants per tour** (per mission beekeeping industry standard; [Mission Beekeeping](https://missionbeekeeping.com/beehive-tour/)) |

### 4.2 Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

| Item | Specification | Source/Supply |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Full bee suit | Full-body, elastic cuffs; sizes XS–3XL | Campus inventory: purchase from Mann Lake or Dadant; minimum 25 suits |
| Veil/hood | Attached to suit or separate round veil; no exposed skin at neck | Full-head veils preferred for visitors |
| Gloves | Leather or thick canvas; extends above wrist | Campus inventory; OR visitors may decline gloves (risk informed) |
| Sturdy closed-toe shoes | Beekeeper or visitor's own; no sandals | Visitor PPE guidance sheet distributed at check-in |
| Bee suit fitting | Allow 10 minutes pre-tour for fitting; guide checks each participant before apiary entry | |

**Suit maintenance:** Inspect all suits for holes at elastic cuffs, zipper function, and veil integrity before each tour. Retire any suit with tears or compromised veil seams. Launder suits after each use with unscented detergent (alarm pheromone from bee venom can persist on fabric and increase defensive behavior).

### 4.3 Pre-Tour Briefing (Mandatory, ~5 Minutes)

Guide delivers the following points before any participant enters the apiary:

1. **Medical screening:** "Does anyone have a known severe allergy to bee stings, or carry an EpiPen for bee sting allergy?" (Individuals answering yes: offer observation hive visit; they do not join the suited tour)
2. **Bee behavior:** "These are gentle bees. They sting only when they feel threatened. We'll move slowly and deliberately — no sudden movements."
3. **Suit check:** Guide inspects each participant's suit closure before entry
4. **What to do if stung:** "Tell me immediately. Stay calm — a single sting is uncomfortable but not dangerous unless you have an allergy. We'll move away from the hive, remove the suit, and remove the stinger by scraping (not pinching)."
5. **Smoker:** Explain purpose of smoker; do not point it directly at faces
6. **Hive tool:** Explain purpose; guide handles all hive tools during beginner tours
7. **"Stay in the cluster"**: Participants stay together as a group; do not wander toward unmanaged areas of the apiary independently

### 4.4 During the Tour

- Maximum of 2 hives opened per tour (beginners); up to 4–6 for educational-level tours
- Guide uses smoker before opening each hive; a puff at the entrance, wait 30 seconds, then gentle smoke under the cover
- Only guide handles frames during public tours; advanced education tours may have trained students under close supervision
- Observe and narrate: brood pattern identification, queen spotting, waggle dance observation
- **Sting event protocol:** If a participant is stung through the suit or at an unprotected area, guide assists with moving away from hives, removing the stinger immediately by scraping with a hive tool or fingernail edge (not pinching, which may inject more venom), and assess for signs of systemic reaction for 20–30 minutes

### 4.5 Post-Tour
- Guide inspects suit return for any bees clinging to fabric; gently brush off before suit removal
- Participants remove suits in designated suit-removal zone, not inside the apiary
- Debrief: 5 minutes of Q&A; distribute contact sheet with MDARD apiary inspector and local beekeeping club contacts if interest expressed

## 5. EpiPen Protocol

### 5.1 Equipment Required
- Minimum **2 EpiPens (epinephrine auto-injectors)** on-site at all times during any live bee programming
- Stored in labeled, easily accessible location at apiary entrance and visitor center — not locked in an office
- Staff trained in EpiPen administration per manufacturer guidance (available annually via online certification)
- Check expiration dates monthly; replace before expiration

### 5.2 Anaphylaxis Response Protocol

**Recognize anaphylaxis (any 2+ of the following within minutes of a sting):**
- Hives, itching, flushing (skin)
- Swelling of lips, tongue, or throat
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing, hoarseness
- Rapid or weak pulse
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
- Dizziness, fainting, or loss of consciousness

**Response steps (perform in order):**
1. **Call 911 immediately** — anaphylaxis is a life-threatening emergency
2. **Administer EpiPen** to outer mid-thigh (through clothing is acceptable); hold for 10 seconds
3. **Lay person flat** with legs elevated (unless breathing is easier sitting); do not allow them to stand or walk
4. **Administer second EpiPen** if no improvement in 5–10 minutes and second dose available
5. **Stay with person** until EMS arrives; document time of sting, time of EpiPen administration, and symptoms
6. **All anaphylaxis events require hospital transport** — epinephrine is a temporary bridge, not a cure; rebound reactions can occur

**Important:** Michigan K-12 schools are required to stock epinephrine under state law; campus programs serving school groups should verify school's own EpiPen policy ([United Educators, 2021](https://www.ue.org/risk-management/enterprise-risk-management/beekeeping-on-campus/)).

### 5.3 Post-Incident
- Complete incident report within 24 hours
- Contact campus legal/risk management
- Review tour protocol and PPE for adequacy
- Notify insurance carrier if medical claim anticipated

## 6. Waivers and Liability

### 6.1 Waiver Requirements
A signed liability waiver is **required** for all participants in:
- Suited apiary tours (adults and minors)
- Any program involving proximity to open hives

**Waiver must include:**
- Description of activities and inherent risks (bee stings, outdoor physical activity)
- Acknowledgment that participant has disclosed known bee sting allergies
- Medical screening confirmation (no known severe allergy, or explicit assumption of risk by EpiPen-carrying individuals)
- Parent/guardian signature for participants under 18
- Emergency contact information

**Waiver does NOT eliminate liability for negligence** (improper equipment, lack of trained staff, inadequate medical protocols). Waivers are a procedural requirement and part of risk documentation, not a complete legal shield. Consult with campus legal counsel on waiver language.

### 6.2 Insurance

Per [United Educators beekeeping on campus guidelines (2021)](https://www.ue.org/risk-management/enterprise-risk-management/beekeeping-on-campus/):
- Verify that the campus general liability insurance covers apiary operations and visitor tours
- Some insurers require endorsement for beekeeping operations; confirm with risk management
- Liability insurance specifically covering apiary visitor programs is available through specialty agricultural insurers
- For observation hive events at off-campus locations: verify coverage extends to external events or arrange event-specific riders
- [University of Florida IFAS guidelines](https://ask.ifas.ufl.edu/publication/MG320) note that some event venues require liability insurance as a condition of bringing live bees on-site

**Michigan apiary registration:** Michigan does not require beekeeper registration; however, maintaining MDARD inspection records and following all state apiary law requirements is best practice for liability defense.

## 7. Accessibility

- Observation hive viewing is fully accessible (no mobility restrictions; behind glass)
- Suited tours: accessible paths to apiary; bee suits can be worn over wheelchairs and mobility aids with guide assistance
- Visual/cognitive accommodations: verbal narration of all visual content; simplified language available for school groups
- Bee allergy accommodation: observation hive alternative is always offered; never pressure individuals to participate in suited tours

## 8. Emergency Response Plan Summary

| Emergency | Immediate Response |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Bee sting (single, no allergy) | Remove stinger; ice; antihistamine (campus first aid kit); monitor 30 min |
| Bee sting (suspected allergy) | EpiPen immediately; 911; lay flat; second EpiPen at 5–10 min if no improvement |
| Mass stinging event (hive disturbance) | Move all participants calmly away from hive area; do not run (vibration increases aggression); remove suits when 50+ feet away |
| Bee suit failure (significant gap) | Guide immediately covers gap with hand; move participant to edge of apiary; remove suit away from open hives |
| Fire/evacuation | Remove bees from open state (close hive); all participants evacuate per campus emergency plan |
