## Overview: Three Distinct Pollinator Zones

The CEA Campus integrates three distinct pollinator systems, each with specific species, purpose, management, and visitor interpretation. Clear species distinction is essential — not just biologically, but in visitor communications. Conflating honey bees with bumble bees with native wild pollinators confuses educational messaging and creates inappropriate management expectations.

| Zone | Primary Species | Location | Purpose | Managed By |
|------|---------------|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Zone 1 — Outdoor Apiary + Rooftop** | Honey bee (*Apis mellifera*) | Ground apiary + rooftop hives | Honey production, education, winter storage demonstrations | Apiary Manager (Amira Bloom / staff) |
| **Zone 2 — Tomato Greenhouse** | Eastern bumble bee (*Bombus impatiens*) | Tomato greenhouse interior | Commercial buzz pollination of greenhouse tomato crop | Greenhouse team; Koppert/Biobest hive vendor |
| **Zone 3 — Native Pollinator Meadow** | Native wild pollinators (150+ species) | Outdoor meadow/prairie plantings | Biodiversity, ecosystem services, native bee habitat, educational transects | Ecologist; minimal intervention |

## Zone 1: Honey Bees (*Apis mellifera*)

### Species Description
The European honey bee — a managed, social colony species living in permanent wooden hive boxes. A single colony contains **20,000–60,000 workers**, one queen, and seasonal drones. Workers are female; drones (male) are present only in spring/summer. The colony overwinters as a tight cluster.

### On-Campus Locations
- **Ground-level apiary:** 5–10 production hives in a dedicated, fenced apiary zone with flight path deflectors (>6-foot hedge or fence within 10 feet of hives to direct flight upward over visitor areas)
- **Rooftop hives:** 2–4 hives on accessible flat roof section, visible from interior campus via viewing platform; provides dramatic educational visual of urban beekeeping

### Purpose
1. **Education:** Primary "ambassador" bee species; observation hive in visitor center; suited tours (see visitor programs)
2. **Honey production:** Campus retail honey, F&B integration, value-added products (beeswax candles, lip balm)
3. **Winter storage connection:** Campus story links to the 4,000-hive indoor storage facility — this is where "Michigan beekeepers' bees come to rest"
4. **Research partnership:** Data colony for MSU Pollinator Performance Center collaboration

### Management
- Full Varroa monitoring program per HBHC 2026 standards (alcohol wash monthly, April–October)
- Annual spring inspection for AFB/EFB
- Seasonal management: spring build-up → honey flow → fall treatment → winter preparation
- Michigan honey labeling per MDARD requirements

### Signage Guidance (Zone 1)
> **"Honey Bees — *Apis mellifera*"**  
> *These golden-striped bees live in carefully managed wooden hives. Each colony contains up to 60,000 workers and one queen. Honey bees are remarkable navigators — foragers can fly up to 5 miles in search of nectar and pollen, communicating direction to their hivemates through the waggle dance.*
>
> *Please stay on the visitor path. Bees are gentle when undisturbed — avoid sudden movements and strong fragrances near the hives.*

## Zone 2: Eastern Bumble Bees (*Bombus impatiens*) — Tomato Greenhouse

### Species Description
The eastern bumble bee (*Bombus impatiens*) is native to eastern North America and is the commercially reared species used for indoor greenhouse pollination in the US and Canada. Commercially reared colonies of 80–125 workers (with a queen and brood) are supplied in custom cardboard/wood hive boxes by certified producers.

> **Critical distinction from honey bees:** Greenhouse tomato requires **buzz pollination** (sonication) — a vibrating, high-frequency behavior in which a bee grabs the tomato flower and vibrates its flight muscles to release pollen. Honey bees (*Apis mellifera*) do **not** perform buzz pollination effectively; bumble bees excel at it. This is why **honey bees are NOT used in tomato greenhouses**, and why the CEA Campus must maintain a strict species separation in visitor communications. Introducing honey bees into a tomato greenhouse would result in zero fruit set.

All commercially grown greenhouse tomatoes in the US rely on bumble bees for pollination — as noted in the [Ohio State University Ohioline fact sheet on bumble bees in tomato greenhouses](https://ohioline.osu.edu/factsheet/ent-0092), confirmed by [MSU Extension's 2019 bumble bee in greenhouse vegetable production article](https://www.canr.msu.edu/news/bumble-bees-in-greenhouse-vegetable-production).

### Commercial Hive Specifications

| Koppert | Natupol Excel | *B. impatiens* | 75–100 | ~9 weeks | 1,000–15,000 sq ft | [ARBICO Organics](https://www.arbico-organics.com/product/natupol-bumblebee-hives/pollination-aids) |
| Koppert | Natupol Excel Startup | *B. impatiens* | 100–125 | 7–8 weeks | 1,000–15,000 sq ft | ARBICO Organics |
| Koppert | Natupol Trio | *B. impatiens* | ~80 | 6–8 weeks | — | [Koppert Global](https://www.koppert.com/natupol-trio/) |
| Biobest | Standard Hive | *B. terrestris* | >80 | 6–8 weeks | — | [Biobest Group](https://www.biobest.com/products/standard-hive) |

> **Michigan-specific:** The [MSU Extension bumble bee article (2019)](https://www.canr.msu.edu/news/bumble-bees-in-greenhouse-vegetable-production) notes colony cost of **$135–$175** per hive (2019 pricing; current prices estimated at $150–$200+). One colony covers a greenhouse of 30 ft × 48 ft to 30 ft × 192 ft. Standard colony density: 7–15 colonies per hectare ([Ohio State, Ohioline](https://ohioline.osu.edu/factsheet/ent-0092)).

**Hive placement:** 4–7 days before flowering begins (Natupol Excel) or at time of flowering (Natupol Excel Startup). Position hives evenly throughout the greenhouse plot, away from direct sunlight or heat sources. Hives include flight holes (two-way and one-way) that allow management of bee flight.

**Pollination verification:** A well-pollinated tomato flower shows a dark "bruise mark" on the anther cone. [Koppert recommends 90–100% bruising on open flowers](https://www.domyown.com/koppert-natupol-class-bumblebee-hive-for-5000-10000-sq-ft-p-11621.html) as the standard sign of adequate pollination activity.

**Replacement cycle:** New colonies are ordered every 6–8 weeks per crop cycle; colonies are not reused (commercial protocol). 12-week lead time on orders from suppliers ([MSU Extension](https://www.canr.msu.edu/news/bumble-bees-in-greenhouse-vegetable-production)).

### Signage Guidance (Zone 2 — Tomato Greenhouse)
> **"Bumble Bees — *Bombus impatiens*"**  
> *These fuzzy, gentle bumble bees are hard at work pollinating our tomatoes. Unlike honey bees, bumble bees "buzz pollinate" — they vibrate their body at a precise frequency to shake pollen loose from tomato flowers. This is the only way tomato flowers release their pollen.*
>
> *You may notice dark marks on tomato flowers — those are pollination "bite marks," proof that a bumble bee visited and did its job!*
>
> *These bees are for greenhouse work only and are different from the honey bees in our outdoor apiary. Please do not disturb the hive boxes — the queen is inside.*

## Zone 3: Native Wild Pollinators — Prairie Meadow

### Species Overview
Michigan hosts more than 450 species of native bees. The campus meadow/prairie planting is designed to support as many of these as possible, particularly:

| Functional Group | Representative Genera | Nesting Habit | Key Plants |
|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|------------|
| Ground-nesting solitary bees | *Andrena*, *Agapostemon*, *Halictus* | Bare soil, south-facing slopes | Willows, spring ephemerals |
| Wood-nesting solitary bees | *Osmia*, *Megachile*, *Xylocopa* | Hollow stems, dead wood, bee hotel | Phacelia, sunflower, coneflower |
| Native bumble bees (wild) | *Bombus griseocollis*, *B. auricomus*, *B. ternarius* | Underground cavities | Clover, monarda, milkweed |
| Sweat bees | *Lasioglossum*, *Halictus* | Soil | Aster, goldenrod |
| Long-tongued bees | *Eulaema*, *Anthophora* | Varied | Deep tubular flowers |

### Campus Meadow Design Principles
- **Native plant palette:** Primarily Michigan-native prairie and meadow species: coneflower (*Echinacea*), black-eyed Susan (*Rudbeckia*), wild bergamot (*Monarda fistulosa*), nodding wild onion (*Allium cernuum*), prairie blazing star (*Liatris pycnostachya*), common milkweed (*Asclepias syriaca*), goldenrod (*Solidago*), New England aster (*Symphyotrichum novae-angliae*)
- **Bloom succession:** Plantings timed for sequential bloom from April through October, providing continuous forage for bees of all tongue lengths
- **Reduced-disturbance management:** Leave dead stems standing through winter (important for stem-nesting bees); mow/cut in spring only; no insecticide applications
- **Bare soil sections:** South-facing, undisturbed soil patches for ground-nesting bees (50+ cm², gentle slope, sandy loam)
- **Bee hotel:** Decorative native bee hotel with varying diameter hollow stems/tubes (4–8 mm diameter for mason bees, 6–10 mm for leafcutter bees); change tubes annually to prevent disease buildup; place in east-facing position to catch morning sun

### Relationship to Honey Bee Apiary
The native meadow is intentionally sited away from the honey bee apiary to reduce competition pressure. Research indicates honey bees can outcompete native bees for floral resources in high-density scenarios. The meadow's native plant palette is specifically chosen to favor native specialist pollinators over generalist honey bees.

### Educational Transect
A mown walking path through the meadow with species identification markers allows guided and self-guided pollinator observation. Design as an "insect observation transect" for school groups: students use hand lenses and identification cards to observe and record wild bees on plants. Connect to citizen science platforms (iNaturalist, Bumble Bee Watch).

### Signage Guidance (Zone 3 — Native Meadow)
> **"Wild Native Pollinators"**  
> *This meadow was designed for Michigan's 450+ species of native wild bees — most of them solitary, non-stinging, and rarely noticed.*
>
> *Ground-nesting bees tunnel into bare soil. Stem-nesting bees seek hollow plant stems. Many of them look nothing like a honey bee — some are metallic green, some are tiny as a grain of rice, some are striped with white.*
>
> *Please stay on the path. The "weedy" look is intentional — dead stems and leaf litter are winter homes for pollinators. What looks like disorder is habitat.*

## Species Distinction at a Glance — Visitor Quick Reference

| Feature | Honey Bee | Bumble Bee (commercial) | Native Wild Bees |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Where you'll see them | Outdoor apiary, rooftop, observation hive | Tomato greenhouse only | Meadow, flowering gardens |
| Colony size | 20,000–60,000 | 80–125 workers | 1 (solitary) or 5–50 (social) |
| Nest type | Wooden hive boxes | Commercial cardboard/wood hive | Soil, hollow stems, wood |
| Honey production | Yes (primary) | No | No |
| Sting risk | Low (suited tours only) | Very low (docile) | Very low (most don't sting) |
| Buzz pollination | No | Yes (tomatoes) | Some species |
| Managed by humans | Yes (all year) | Yes (6–8 weeks per crop) | No intervention |

## Signage Design Principles

1. **Plain language:** All signs at approximately grade 6 reading level for public zones; grade 8–10 for educational trail signs
2. **Visual first:** Each sign features a large, accurate photograph or illustration of the species — not a generic "bee" clip art
3. **"This is NOT the same bee" messaging:** At Zone 2 greenhouse entrance, clear signage: "Bumble bees here — not the same as the honey bees in the outdoor apiary"
4. **No-touch reminders near all hive areas:** Universal icon plus brief text; no scare language
5. **Multilingual:** English/Spanish at minimum for visitor center and apiary tour zones
6. **QR codes:** Link to extended digital content (MSU Pollinator Performance Center, iNaturalist Michigan bee guide, MBA website)
