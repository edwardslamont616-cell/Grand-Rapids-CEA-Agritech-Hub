## Program Architecture Overview

The CEA Campus apiary offers three interlocking program tracks, each with distinct audiences, pricing models, and educational goals. All tracks share the campus's central narrative: *pollinators are not separate from food — they are food*.

| Track | Audience | Format | Duration | Fee |
|-------|---------|--------|----------|-----|
| **Track 1 — Pollinator Discovery** | K–5 school groups; families with young children | Guided meadow walk + observation hive | 45–60 min | $8–$12/student; $10–$15/adult |
| **Track 2 — The Beekeeper Experience** | Adults; upper-middle school (grades 6+); agritourism guests | Suited apiary tour + honey tasting | 90 min | $35–$50/person |
| **Track 3 — Deeper Dives** | High school, community college, adult learners; beekeeper clubs | Half-day or full-day workshops | 3–6 hrs | $75–$150/person |

## Track 1: Pollinator Discovery
### TAG Analysis
- **Task:** Design a safe, joyful, science-rooted pollinator experience for young children and families that does not require bee suits or any sting risk
- **Audience:** K–5 elementary school groups (ages 5–11); families with children during public hours
- **Goal:** Children leave with (1) basic understanding of why pollinators matter, (2) ability to identify honey bees, bumble bees, and 2–3 native bee types, (3) genuine excitement — not fear — about bees

### BAB Structure

**Before (the problem):** Most children encounter bees only in the context of fear — a bee near a picnic is a hazard, not a neighbor. School science standards cover pollination abstractly without making it real. And "bees are dying" is a frightening concept without agency.

**After (the transformation):** A child who watches a bee perform the waggle dance in an observation hive, who finds a native mason bee tunnel in a bee hotel, who tastes clover honey and knows which flower it came from — that child has a relationship with pollinators. They carry that forward.

**Bridge (the program):**

### Session Flow (60 minutes)

**Station 1: The Big Picture (10 min) — Visitor Center or Pavilion**
- "One out of every three bites of food you eat was pollinated by a bee or other insect."
- Show: giant poster or projection of a plate of food with/without pollinators (the visual is striking: half the plate disappears)
- Introduce the three bee types they'll meet today (honey bee, bumble bee, native solitary bee)
- Introduce the "Pollinator Passport" — a simple illustrated card each student carries; stamp/sticker at each station

**Station 2: The Observation Hive (15 min) — Visitor Center**
- 4–6 frame permanent observation hive behind glass
- Guide narrates: "Can you find the queen?" (Queen marked with paint; color coded by year)
- "What are those bees doing on the comb? That's the waggle dance — they're giving directions to the flower patch"
- "These cells are full of honey. This cell has a bee egg inside — smaller than a grain of rice"
- Age-appropriate Q&A: why do bees have stripes? Do all bees sting? Why do bees make honey?
- **Key message:** "These bees can't reach you through the glass — you are completely safe. They don't know you're here."

**Station 3: Bee Hotel and Native Meadow Walk (20 min) — Outdoor Meadow**
- "Most Michigan bees don't live in hives at all — they live alone"
- Show the bee hotel: point out mason bee tubes, leafcutter bee chambers
- Walk to 3 flagged meadow plants; identify the "bee magnet" species
- Observe native bees on flowers with hand lenses (provided)
- Stamp/sticker: students draw a native bee they observed in their Pollinator Passport
- **Key message:** "Wild bees don't have a hive to protect — they almost never sting"

**Station 4: Honey Tasting + Wrap-Up (15 min) — Pavilion**
- Three honey varieties tasted: clover (light), wildflower (medium), buckwheat (dark)
- Connect flavor to flower: "The bees flew 2 miles to find clover flowers to make this honey"
- Connection back to food: point to the tomato greenhouse visible from pavilion — "Those tomatoes were pollinated by bumble bees, not honey bees. Different bee, different job"
- Distribute bee-related seed packet (sunflower or phacelia) — "Plant this; a bee will find it"
- Educator debrief: 5 minutes on how to continue at school (Michigan Pollinator Pathway plant guide; iNaturalist app)

### K–12 Curriculum Alignment
- Next Generation Science Standards: LS2-2 (Ecosystem dynamics, functioning, and resilience); LS2-4 (Social interactions and group behavior); LS4-6 (Biodiversity and humans)
- Michigan Science Standards: aligned at grade 3 (life cycles, ecosystems) through grade 8 (ecosystems, biodiversity)
- Connection to Prof. Elena Cruz's CEA Campus education curriculum

### Safety (Track 1)
- No bee suits; no proximity to open hives
- Students maintain ≥15 feet from outdoor apiary fence at all times during self-guided meadow walk
- Pre-program teacher/parent form asks about bee sting allergy; allergic students observe only (no meadow); teacher contacts Amira Bloom 2 weeks before visit
- Observation hive glass panel checked for integrity before each school group

## Track 2: The Beekeeper Experience
### TAG Analysis
- **Task:** Deliver an immersive, hands-on apiary tour that makes adult visitors feel the real craft of beekeeping — not a gimmick, but a legitimate skill
- **Audience:** Adults (18+); families with older children (12+); agritourism guests; corporate team-building groups; foodies
- **Goal:** Participants leave having opened a hive, observed living bees at close range, and understood the connection between colony health, pollination, and food — with a product in hand (honey sample, optional retail purchase)

### Session Flow (90 minutes)

**Part 1 — Introduction (20 min) — Pavilion**
- Welcome and context: "You're about to look inside a colony of 50,000 bees — one of the most complex social organisms on earth"
- Brief safety briefing (per Visitor Safety Plan)
- Suit fitting: everyone in full bee suits; guide inspects all closures
- Introduce smoker: guide demonstrates, explains physiology of smoke's calming effect (masks alarm pheromone; mimics forest fire instinct)
- Show hive tool; explain propolis ("bee glue") and why the hive is so tightly sealed

**Part 2 — In the Apiary (50 min) — Outdoor Apiary**
- Maximum 20 participants, guided by lead guide + one assistant
- Open 2 hives; narrate frame-by-frame inspection: "This is capped honey — see how it's a lighter tan? This is capped brood — darker, flatter. This is a polished queen cup — the bees are thinking about swarming"
- Queen spotting activity (queen marked): "The first person to spot the queen gets a jar of honey"
- Observe waggle dance if present; "She just came back from flowers and is giving directions"
- Demonstrate Varroa monitoring (show alcohol wash jar; count mites if present; explain the threat)
- Optional hands-on: guided frame handling for willing participants (lead guide supervises individually)
- Connection to winter storage: "These hives might spend this winter in our indoor storage facility — 4,000 colonies from Michigan beekeepers, kept at 40°F until spring"

**Part 3 — Honey Tasting + Retail (20 min) — Pavilion or Visitor Center**
- Taste 2–3 campus honey varieties; discuss terroir, seasonality, bee behavior behind each
- Optional retail: campus honey, beeswax products, native bee seed mixes
- Q&A: What do I need to start beekeeping? Provide MBA local club list, MSU Extension beginner resources
- Debrief card: "Before you leave, write one thing you'll do differently after meeting our bees today"

### Pricing Rationale
At $35–$50/person × 20 participants = **$700–$1,000 per tour**.  
Two tours per day (morning + afternoon on busy days, seasonal) = $1,400–$2,000/day.  
Estimated 150–200 tour days/year = **$210,000–$400,000/year gross** from guided tours alone — supporting program staff costs.

### Safety (Track 2)
Per full visitor safety plan: suits required; EpiPen present; signed waivers; max 20 per group; pre-screening for sting allergy.

## Track 3: Deeper Dives — Workshops and Educational Programs
### TAG Analysis
- **Task:** Provide structured, technical education for beekeeping students, club members, high school science programs, and adult learners
- **Audience:** Serious beginner-to-intermediate beekeepers; high school and community college biology students; MBA member groups; corporate sustainability programs
- **Goal:** Measurable knowledge transfer; connection to MSU Extension resources; pathway to ongoing beekeeper engagement with the facility

### Workshop Menu

**Workshop 3A: "Introduction to Beekeeping" (3 hours, $75/person, max 15)**
- Beekeeping biology: colony life cycle, caste roles, seasonal calendar
- Equipment overview: hands-on with smoker, hive tool, protective gear
- Hive inspection practice: open 2–4 hives in small groups with close supervision
- Varroa monitoring: alcohol wash demonstration, students perform wash on sample colony
- Take-home: Michigan beekeeping reference packet (MDARD regulations; MBA club list; MSU Extension resources)
- Audience: complete beginners; school group (HS biology class); corporate sustainability day

**Workshop 3B: "Colony Health and Varroa Management" (half-day, $100/person, max 12)**
- HBHC Varroa Management Guide deep-dive
- Monitoring methods: alcohol wash, sticky board, sugar roll — participants practice each
- Treatment options: Apivar, oxalic acid, formic acid — label reading, application windows, resistance management
- Nosema identification: microscopy demo
- AFB/EFB identification: photo guide, ropiness test demo
- Audience: second-year beekeepers; MBA chapter programs; sideline operations building toward commercial

**Workshop 3C: "Indoor Winter Storage — Behind the Scenes" (2 hours, $50/person, seasonal Oct–Feb only)**
- Tour of the indoor storage facility (suited, red lights, quiet)
- Explain BOD parameters (temperature, humidity, CO₂, darkness)
- View stacked pallets of stored hives; monitor readout
- Discussion: why October intake matters; what the brood break means for Varroa; what to look for at spring release
- Audience: Michigan commercial and sideliner beekeepers; MBA members; agronomy students; agricultural tourism groups

**Workshop 3D: K–12 Advanced (customized, $8–$15/student, minimum 20)**
- Designed with Prof. Elena Cruz / CEA Campus Education Director
- Integrates with science standards (NGSS, Michigan)
- Full year sequences available: "Adopt a Hive" program — class monitors one hive remotely via hive sensor data (temperature, humidity, weight) plus 3 on-site visits across the season
- Audience: 5th–12th grade science classes; 4-H groups; FFA chapters

## Youth Safety Notes (All Tracks)
Per [SARE YENC12-052 Youth Beekeeping Pilot](https://projects.sare.org/sare_project/yenc12-052/) and subsequent [SARE YENC14-079 National Model](https://projects.sare.org/sare_project/yenc14-079/):
- Youth beekeeping is most successful when structured, incremental, and hands-on from week one
- Equipment: properly fitting suits (not adult suits rolled up); provide bee-specific hats/veils if full suits not available
- Knowledge-before-hive: introduce bee biology, identification, and behavior before first hive opening
- Emotional literacy: normalize fear ("Even experienced beekeepers get nervous sometimes"); celebrate small wins ("You found the queen — that's something many adults can't do")
- Parent/guardian communication: inform of activity content and safety protocols; collect medical information in advance
- "Opt-out always available": no participant — adult or youth — is ever pressured to open a hive or hold a frame
