## 1. Michigan Honey Bee Industry — Baseline Data

### 1.1 Colony Counts

According to the [USDA NASS Michigan Honey Production News Release (March 2025)](https://www.nass.usda.gov/Statistics_by_State/Michigan/Publications/Current_News_Release/2025/nr2517mi.pdf), Michigan had **82,000 honey-producing colonies in 2024**, producing 5.00 million pounds of honey, valued at $15.2 million ($3.04/lb). Michigan ranked **sixth nationally in honey production volume**.

The [WKAR reporting (February 2026)](https://www.wkar.org/wkar-news/2026-02-27/michigan-beekeepers-enter-2026-season-after-record-winter-losses-nationwide) confirmed the 82,000-colony figure for honey-producing colonies.

> **Note on the 67,500 figure:** The project context references ~67,500 "commercial" hives. This likely refers to the subset of the 82,000 total colonies managed by commercial and sideliner operations (5+ hives), which have historically represented the largest share of Michigan's colony stock. The Michigan Pollinator Protection Plan notes that the [exact number of beekeepers is unknown, as Michigan has not maintained a mandatory apiary registry since 1993](https://www.michigan.gov/-/media/Project/Websites/mdard/documents/pesticide-plant-pest/pesticide/Communication-strategies-for-reducing-pesticide-risk-for-managed-pollinators-in-michigan.pdf). In 1992, Michigan registered ~2,500 apiaries for 100,000+ colonies. Both the 67,500 and 82,000 figures are used in the scenario tables below.

### 1.2 MDARD Registry Status

Michigan currently does **not** maintain a mandatory beekeeper registry or require a license. The [MDARD Apiary Inspections page](https://www.michigan.gov/mdard/plant-pest/plant-health/apiary-inspections) confirms inspections are available on request (contact: Bryan Webster, 517-719-1693, WebsterB3@Michigan.gov) and are primarily used for interstate shipment health certification. The absence of a registry makes precise beekeeper segmentation difficult; survey outreach (see outbound drafts) is essential for first-hand market intelligence.

### 1.3 Michigan Beekeepers Association (MBA)

The [Michigan Beekeepers Association](https://www.michiganbees.org) is the oldest continuously operating agricultural organization in Michigan. The MBA coordinates with 20+ local clubs across the state and serves both commercial and hobbyist members. MBA co-hosts annual spring conferences with MSU Extension, making it the primary beekeeper community channel for outreach (see survey invitation draft).

### 1.4 Scale Segments

| Segment | Hive Range | Estimate % of Colonies | Notes |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|-------|
| Hobbyist (backyard) | 1–4 hives | ~25% | Not target customer for storage |
| Sideliner | 5–99 hives | ~35% | Potential customers if transport costs meaningful |
| Commercial | 100+ hives | ~40% | Primary target for winter storage |

Commercial operations account for the majority of hive volume. Facebook discussions reference [Michigan commercial beekeepers with ~5,000 hives](https://www.facebook.com/groups/219190295441261/posts/1732077157485893/), indicating a small number of large operations driving much of the total stock.

## 2. Current Winter Behavior Alternatives

Michigan commercial beekeepers currently face three primary wintering pathways:

### Option A: California Holding Yards (Almond Pollination)
The most financially significant option. ~2.7–2.8 million colonies from across the US (more than the entire US managed stock) are transported to California each winter for almond bloom (late January – mid-March). [USDA NASS Cost of Pollination, February 2026](https://esmis.nal.usda.gov/sites/default/release-files/795775/cospol25.pdf) confirmed the 2025 average fee was **$209/colony**, up 15% from $181 in 2024. Michigan commercial operations that can meet the minimum colony strength requirements (7–9 frames average) have strong financial incentive to send hives to California.

**Tradeoff:** California almond transport involves significant costs, Varroa reinfestation risk at high-density holding yards, and unpredictable winter loss (see Section 3). Winter 2024–25 saw record losses.

### Option B: Outdoor Michigan Wintering
Some beekeepers — particularly smaller operations and those with cold-hardy local genetics — winter outdoors in Michigan. MSU's beekeeping guidelines and the University of Minnesota Bee Lab both provide outdoor wintering protocols. This approach carries high weather-dependent risk: [national commercial winter losses hit 40.7% in 2024–25](https://apiaryinspectors.org/US-beekeeping-survey-24-25), the worst on record. Michigan-specific losses tracked by WKAR reporting show the state is directly impacted by these national trends.

### Option C: Indoor Storage (Idaho/Western US)
An estimated [500,000 colonies nationwide are stored in indoor climate-controlled facilities](https://nationalnutgrower.com/article/indoor-storage-of-honeybees/), primarily in Idaho and adjacent states. This is currently unavailable in Michigan at commercial scale. Beekeepers who want this option must ship hives out-of-state.

**The gap this facility fills:** There is no Michigan commercial-scale indoor winter storage facility. The MSU facility (40 hives) is a research installation. Grand Rapids CEA Hub would be the first.

## 4. Winter Loss Context — The Urgency Driver

National winter losses create urgent and growing demand for better wintering solutions:

| Season | National Winter Loss Rate | Commercial Beekeeper Loss | Source |
|--------|--------------------------|--------------------------|--------|
| 2024–2025 | **40.2%** (highest on record) | **40.7%** (highest on record) | [AIA/Auburn 2025](https://apiaryinspectors.org/US-beekeeping-survey-24-25) |
| 17-year running average | 29.3% | 28.2% | AIA 2025 |
| 2023–2024 | 37.3% | — | AIA 2025 |
| Annual losses (full year) 2024–25 | **55.6%** (highest ever) | **56.2%** | AIA 2025 |

Michigan beekeepers entered the 2026 season after record nationwide losses; [WKAR reporting (February 2026)](https://www.wkar.org/wkar-news/2026-02-27/michigan-beekeepers-enter-2026-season-after-record-winter-losses-nationwide) confirmed Michigan is tracking this national trend.

**The value proposition:** Controlled indoor storage at the target parameters (38–42°F, 50–65% RH, <1,500 ppm CO₂, complete darkness) can achieve 76–82% survival rates ([USDA ARS, 2020](https://www.ars.usda.gov/news-events/news/research-news/2020/helping-honey-bees-make-it-through-winter-with-early-cold-storage/)) vs. the 59.3% national average for outdoor commercial wintering in 2024–25. For a Michigan commercial operator with 500 hives, moving from 59% to 82% survival saves approximately **115 colonies** — at replacement cost of $150–$250/nuc, that is **$17,250–$28,750 in saved replacement costs alone**, dwarfing a $75,000 storage fee at $150/hive × 500 hives.

## 5. Competitor Landscape

| Option | Location | Capacity | Price | Notes |

| Idaho commercial operators | ID/adjacent states | Very large | ~$100–$205/hive | [DeGrandi-Hoffman data](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/650342507631075013d25a2c/t/664636c97fc2341121d317b8/1715877582458/Indoor+Storage+Guide+V+2.0+FINAL+WEBSITE_reduced_Optimized.pdf) cites $205/colony seasonal cost; long-haul transport required from MI |

| California holding yards | CA | Very large | Offset by $209 almond fee | High density; Varroa reinfestation risk; long-distance trucking |
| MSU facility | East Lansing | 40 hives | Research only | Not commercial; not a competitor |
| **CEA Hub facility (proposed)** | **Grand Rapids** | **4,000 Phase 1** | **$100–$150/hive** | **Only Michigan commercial option; no long-haul transport** |

**Competitive advantage:** Proximity to Michigan beekeepers eliminates ~2,000-mile roundtrip transport to Idaho. Transport savings alone of $15–$50/hive (regional) or $30–$80/hive (long-haul estimate) effectively reduce the net cost of local storage vs. Idaho.

## 6. Customer Profile and Willingness to Pay

**Primary target customers:**
- Michigan commercial operators (100–5,000+ hives) who currently send hives to California or outdoor winter in Michigan
- Sideliner operations (25–99 hives) who lose large fractions to outdoor wintering and cannot easily self-build cold storage
- Out-of-state beekeepers who pass through Michigan corridor en route between North Dakota, Ohio, or Wisconsin and California

**Estimated willingness to pay:**
- Idaho commercial storage: ~$100–$205/season including facilities ([Project Apis m. Guide](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/650342507631075013d25a2c/t/664636c97fc2341121d317b8/1715877582458/Indoor+Storage+Guide+V+2.0+FINAL+WEBSITE_reduced_Optimized.pdf))
- Almond pollination fee premium for ≥9-frame colonies: ~6% = ~$12.54/hive ([USDA ARS 2020](https://www.ars.usda.gov/news-events/news/research-news/2020/helping-honey-bees-make-it-through-winter-with-early-cold-storage/))
- Michigan-based beekeeper avoiding 2,000-mile trucking: transport savings may justify premium over Idaho rates
- Recommended rate for Phase 1: **$100–$125/hive** to establish market, scaling to $150 as facility demonstrates superior survival data

**Survey needed:** See outbound draft `beekeeper_survey_invitation.md` for 20–30 beekeeper willingness-to-pay survey design.

## 7. Strategic Implications

1. **Early October intake** is the key differentiator vs. traditional November intake — 6% higher survival, stronger February colonies, premium pollination pricing eligibility ([USDA ARS 2020](https://www.ars.usda.gov/news-events/news/research-news/2020/helping-honey-bees-make-it-through-winter-with-early-cold-storage/)).
2. **Data partnership with MSU** elevates the facility beyond a commercial service to a regional research node — enabling Michigan-specific survival data publication that builds trust with beekeepers.
3. **MBA channel partnership:** The Michigan Beekeepers Association annual spring conference (co-hosted with MSU Extension) is the primary outreach venue. MBA membership encompasses the full spectrum from hobbyist to commercial.
4. **Beekeeper network effect:** The facility becomes a gathering place, not just storage — building the beekeeper-network draw envisioned in project context.
