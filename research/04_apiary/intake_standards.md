# Intake Standards: Colony Health Requirements for Indoor Winter Storage
### CEA Campus Agritech Hub — Grand Rapids, Michigan
**Prepared by:** Amira Bloom, Apiary Specialist & Pollination Ecologist  
**CEA Campus Design Council**  
**Date:** 2026  
**Framework:** CARE  
**Status:** Draft for Design Council and Regulatory Review

---

## 1. Philosophy: The Hotel, Not the Hospital

Indoor winter storage cannot salvage a colony that enters in poor condition. As established practice in commercial Idaho operations and affirmed by [USDA NASS and Project Apis m. research](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/650342507631075013d25a2c/t/664636c97fc2341121d317b8/1715877582458/Indoor+Storage+Guide+V+2.0+FINAL+WEBSITE_reduced_Optimized.pdf): **the condition of the colony going in determines its condition coming out.** Beekeepers who invest in pre-storage colony preparation — timely Varroa treatment, queen verification, adequate honey stores — will be rewarded with spring colonies strong enough to reach premium pollination pricing (≥9 frames) or meet Michigan spring build-up.

This facility applies a **pass/hold/reject** intake protocol to protect all stored colonies (a diseased hive endangers neighbors) and protect beekeeper investment (paying $100–$150 for storage of a colony destined to die wastes the fee).

---

## 2. Pre-Intake Preparation Timeline (Beekeeper Responsibility)

**By September 15:**
- Varroa alcohol wash performed on all storage-destined colonies
- Varroa load must be ≤2% (≤6 mites per 300 bees) per [Honey Bee Health Coalition 2026 Guidelines](https://honeybeehealthcoalition.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/HBHC-Guide_Varroa-Mgmt_8thEd-082422.pdf)
- If >2%: treat immediately with appropriate miticide; re-test 3–5 weeks post-treatment
- Preferred treatment in broodless window: Oxalic acid vaporization (Api-Bioxal®) or Apivar® strips (50–63 days); both are highly effective during the pre-cluster population-decrease phase

**By October 1:**
- Queen verified laying (egg presence confirms living queen)
- Colony weight: double-deep box minimum 70–80 lbs winter stores (approximately 60 lbs honey = 6–8 frames capped honey equivalent)
- Post-treatment Varroa retest: must confirm <2% before scheduling intake
- Any colonies queenless or underweight: combine with stronger colony; do NOT store alone

**By Intake Date (October 15 – November 15):**
- Hive entrance screened and reducted for transport
- Hive boxes strapped together (transport straps or ratchet straps)
- Supers removed; no open or partially-filled supers in storage
- Colony records provided to facility (beekeeper name, colony ID, most recent Varroa count + date, treatment history)

## 3. Varroa Mite Standards

### 3.1 Threshold

| Season Phase | Intake Threshold (Alcohol Wash) | Action if Exceeded |
|-------------|--------------------------------|-------------------|
| Pre-intake (by Sept 15) | **≤2%** (≤6 mites/300 bees) | Treat; re-test before intake scheduling |
| On-site intake sampling (spot check) | ≤3% (6–9 mites/300 bees) | Hold in staging; notify beekeeper; return to owner if >3% |
| Reject threshold | >3% at intake wash | Colony returned to beekeeper; no storage |

**Source:** [Honey Bee Health Coalition (HBHC) Tools for Varroa Management, 8th Edition (2022)](https://honeybeehealthcoalition.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/HBHC-Guide_Varroa-Mgmt_8thEd-082422.pdf) — the primary US reference for mite thresholds. The 2026 HBHC update maintains the 2% threshold for July–October as the standard to protect the winter bee cohort, and clarifies that 2% is also appropriate for early spring ([VarroaVault, 2025](https://varroavault.com/honey-bee-health-coalition-varroa-guidelines)).

> **Why this matters:** HBHC data confirms that winter colony losses increase substantially above 3% infestation. The [Project Apis m. Idaho study](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/650342507631075013d25a2c/t/664636c97fc2341121d317b8/1715877582458/Indoor+Storage+Guide+V+2.0+FINAL+WEBSITE_reduced_Optimized.pdf) showed that high Varroa (>8 mites/100 bees in September) predicted dead or weak colonies by October before they could even enter storage. This facility's intake standard directly operationalizes the research finding that pre-storage Varroa control is the #1 predictor of successful overwintering.

### 3.2 Monitoring Method: Alcohol Wash (Gold Standard)

**Protocol:**
1. Collect ~300 adult bees (≈½ cup) from frames adjacent to brood nest, avoiding queen
2. Place bees in wide-mouth jar with #8 hardware cloth lid
3. Add alcohol (isopropyl, ethyl, or ≥40% windshield washer fluid) to cover bees
4. Vigorously swirl ≥1 minute
5. Pour through mesh screen into white tray; bees trapped, mites pass through
6. Repeat wash with fresh alcohol
7. Count mites; calculate: (mite count ÷ 300) × 100 = % infestation

**Alternative (acceptable for pre-documentation):** Sugar roll provides lower accuracy (30–40% lower sensitivity than alcohol wash per HBHC 2026); acceptable for beekeeper trend monitoring between tests, but **alcohol wash is required for intake threshold decisions** ([HBHC 2022](https://honeybeehealthcoalition.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/HBHC-Guide_Varroa-Mgmt_8thEd-082422.pdf)).

**Sticky boards:** Not acceptable as the sole monitoring method; use only as supplemental trend indicator ([HBHC 2026 update](https://varroavault.com/honey-bee-health-coalition-varroa-guidelines)).

### 3.3 On-Site Sampling Protocol

- Facility staff perform intake spot-check alcohol washes on a minimum of **3 hives per truck load** (≥1 per 50 hives), chosen randomly
- If any sampled hive exceeds 3%: full truck load held in external staging; beekeeper contacted; individual colony testing conducted
- A dedicated testing station at the intake vestibule with supplies (jars, hardware cloth, isopropyl alcohol, white trays, headlamps) is part of facility design

## 4. Nosema Screening

### 4.1 Background

*Nosema ceranae* is an intracellular microsporidian fungal parasite affecting adult bees' gut epithelium. It has largely displaced *Nosema apis* in North American apiaries. Clinical signs include reduced forager lifespan, impaired fat body development, and colony dwindling — often without dramatic field symptoms in autumn.

### 4.2 Recommended Screening Method

**Microscopy (400× light microscopy):**
- Pool 60 bees (abdomens) per colony; grind in water; examine under 400× phase-contrast for Nosema spores
- Count spores per bee; threshold: >1 million spores/bee = clinically significant
- Inexpensive; suitable for field labs or extension partners

**PCR (gold standard for species confirmation):**
- Multiplex PCR distinguishes *N. ceranae* from *N. apis* (important as treatment strategy may differ)
- [Detection methods review (Microorganisms, 2025)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12654195/) confirms PCR provides accurate species-level identification; qPCR offers quantification
- For this facility: PCR screening is recommended for any colony flagged by microscopy or exhibiting dysentery signs (fecal spotting on hive front)

### 4.3 Intake Threshold

| Finding | Action |
|---------|--------|
| No spores by microscopy | Accept (pass) |
| 1–3 million spores/bee | Accept with monitoring flag; check at exit |
| >3 million spores/bee | Hold; consult with facility manager; consider reject for storage (high load will worsen in cluster) |
| Dysentery signs visible on hive exterior | Reject; fecal contamination risk to stored neighbors |

> **Note:** Fumagillin (previously the standard Nosema treatment) is not currently registered for use in honey bees in the United States. Management options are limited to strong colony nutrition, maintaining low Varroa loads (co-infection synergy), and requeening. This facility cannot "treat" Nosema on-site; prevention through pre-storage screening and beekeeper education is the primary strategy.

### 4.4 Screening Responsibility

Given facility scale (4,000 hives), full Nosema testing of every colony is impractical. Protocol:
- Beekeeper self-certification: attest no visible dysentery signs, recent colony dwindling, or Nosema history in the current season
- Facility performs microscopy screen on ≥5% of intake pallets each week (or any flagged colony)
- Partnership with MSU Extension or a diagnostic lab (MSU Plant and Pest Diagnostics) for any PCR referral

## 5. American Foulbrood (AFB) Inspection

### 5.1 What It Is

American Foulbrood (*Paenibacillus larvae*) is the most severe bacterial disease of honey bee brood. It produces durable spores that persist in equipment for decades. There is **no treatment that eliminates AFB from infected equipment** — infected colonies and equipment must be burned per state law in most jurisdictions.

### 5.2 Diagnostic Signs

| Sign | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Sunken, greasy cappings | Dark, wet-looking; sometimes punctured |
| "Ropiness" test | Insert twig/matchstick; infected larvae rope ≥2 cm on extraction |
| Pepper-pot brood | Sporadic empty/dead cells mixed with healthy capped brood |
| Odor | Distinctive glue-like/rotting smell |
| Color | Infected larvae brown to black, dessicated scale in cell |

### 5.3 Intake AFB Protocol

**Inspection by:** Trained facility staff or MDARD apiary inspector.
- MDARD offers apiary inspections by request to facilitate interstate shipment; contact Bryan Webster, 517-719-1693, WebsterB3@Michigan.gov ([MDARD](https://www.michigan.gov/mdard/plant-pest/plant-health/apiary-inspections))
- [Michigan Apiary Law, Act 412 of 1976](https://www.michigan.gov/mdard/plant-pest/plant-health/apiary-inspections) authorizes MDARD to impose quarantine on diseased equipment

**Protocol:**
1. Visual inspection of ≥2 brood frames per hive at intake (limited to 10% sample of load if pre-certified)
2. Ropiness test on any suspect cappings
3. **Immediate quarantine and isolation** of any AFB-positive hive; do NOT place near other hives
4. Notify MDARD; beekeeper must be notified and comply with state disease control requirements (likely destruction of infected equipment per Act 412)
5. Zero tolerance: no AFB-positive colony enters the storage building under any circumstance

**Biosecurity note:** AFB spores on contaminated hive tools can transfer the disease. All inspection tools (hive tools, gloves) must be flame-sterilized or disinfected with bleach solution (0.5% sodium hypochlorite) between hive inspections.

### 5.4 APHIS National Honey Bee Survey

The USDA APHIS [National Honey Bee Survey (NHBS)](https://www.usbeedata.org/state_reports/) has been conducted since 2009 to monitor colony health, pest and disease levels, and invasive threats including AFB, Small Hive Beetle, Tropilaelaps, and Varroa. Michigan-specific state reports are available via the NHBS portal. The [ArcGIS AFB inspection summary (2025)](https://experience.arcgis.com/experience/9d6f944c376345b2bd1f066ff519b032/page/American-Fouldbrood) provides county-level apiary inspection data. Staff should monitor this resource for local AFB prevalence.

## 6. European Foulbrood (EFB) Inspection

### 6.1 What It Is

European Foulbrood (*Melissococcus plutonius*) is a bacterial disease of young larvae, typically less severe than AFB. Unlike AFB, colonies can sometimes recover from mild EFB with strong nectar flows; oxytetracycline (Terramycin) has historically been used for treatment but resistance is a growing concern.

### 6.2 Diagnostic Signs

| Sign | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Twisted/melted larvae | Larva twisted in cell, often dead before capping |
| Color | Yellow to brown (not as dark as AFB) |
| No ropiness | Does not rope on probe test |
| Odor | Sour smell |

### 6.3 Intake EFB Protocol

| Finding | Action |
|---------|--------|
| No EFB signs | Accept |
| Mild EFB signs (1–2 cells in a frame) | Hold; consult with beekeeper; document; may accept with monitoring if colony otherwise strong |
| Moderate to severe EFB | Reject from storage; recommend beekeeper consult with MDARD inspector and potentially treat before next season |

## 7. Colony Strength Standards

| Parameter | Minimum for Acceptance | Target | Notes |
|-----------|----------------------|--------|-------|
| Frames of bees (adult population) | ≥8 frames | ≥9–10 frames | <8 frames = high mortality risk in storage |
| Winter honey stores | ≥60 lbs (6–8 frames capped honey) | 80+ lbs | Undersized colonies starve in storage without feeding |
| Queen status | Present; eggs visible or recent brood | Actively laying | Queenless colony: combine; do not store alone |
| Brood pattern | Not required for dormancy | — | Brood should cease naturally within 2–3 weeks of storage |

## 8. Reject Policy and Appeal Process

| Category | Action | Beekeeper Resolution |
|----------|--------|---------------------|
| Varroa >3% (intake wash) | Reject from storage; return same truck or hold 24 hrs | Treat; re-test; may reapply for available space |
| AFB positive | Reject; quarantine; MDARD notification | Comply with state disease law; no re-entry of affected equipment |
| EFB moderate/severe | Reject from storage | Treat with veterinary authorization; re-apply next season |
| Nosema >3M spores/bee | Hold or reject at manager's discretion | Monitor; nutrition support; reapply next season |
| Insufficient colony strength (<8 frames) | Reject | Combine colonies to meet threshold; reapply |
| Missing documentation | Hold pending documentation | 24-hour cure period; documents provided = proceed |

**No storage fee charged for rejected colonies.**  
**No partial refund for colonies that die during storage from pre-existing conditions not identified at intake.** (Beekeeper self-certification provides attestation that pre-intake conditions were met; facility assumes no liability for colonies that fail due to Varroa loads underreported at intake.)

---

## 9. Record-Keeping

For each stored colony:
- Beekeeper name, contact, and colony ID
- Intake date and weight/strength assessment
- Varroa count (method, date, result)
- Nosema status if tested
- AFB/EFB inspection result
- Any notes (weak, queenless flag at intake, etc.)
- Release date and colony condition at exit

Records retained for minimum 3 years. Data shared in anonymized form with MSU Pollinator Performance Center for survival rate research.
