Dear Bryan,

My name is Amira Bloom, and I serve as the Apiary Specialist and Pollination Ecologist for a project development team working on a controlled-environment agriculture campus in Grand Rapids, Michigan. Among our core proposed facilities is a 4,000-hive climate-controlled indoor winter honey bee storage operation — the first of its kind at commercial scale in Michigan.

As we develop market analysis, regulatory compliance plans, and intake biosecurity protocols, your office is a critical resource. I am writing with four specific requests and questions.

**1. Michigan Apiary Data and Beekeeper Population**

The Michigan Pollinator Protection Plan notes that Michigan has not maintained a mandatory apiary registry since 1993, when approximately 2,500 apiaries totaling 100,000+ colonies were on file. USDA NASS currently reports 82,000 honey-producing colonies in Michigan (2024). We are working to understand the current distribution of commercial and sideliner operations — particularly those managing 25+ hives — to assess the addressable market for winter storage services.

*Request:* Does MDARD currently collect any voluntary registration data, participate in any statewide beekeeper surveys, or have access to affiliated databases (Apiary Inspectors of America, Project Apis m., or National Honey Board) that could inform Michigan beekeeper segmentation by scale? Even approximate regional distribution would be valuable for our facility location analysis.

**2. Regulatory Guidance: Indoor Bee Storage Operations**

We are designing a commercial operation that will receive hives from Michigan beekeepers (and potentially out-of-state operators) in October/November, store them in a climate-controlled facility over winter, and release them in February/March — many headed to California for almond pollination.

*Request:* What MDARD permits, licenses, or inspections apply to this type of operation? Specifically:
- Is a facility receiving, storing, and releasing honey bee colonies subject to any state agricultural operation license beyond standard business licensing?
- What documentation (health certificates, inspection records) is required for hives entering from other Michigan locations? From out-of-state?
- What documentation is required for hives exiting Michigan to California or other states post-storage?
- Does Michigan's Apiary Law, Act 412 of 1976 have any provisions that would apply to a commercial storage operation of this scale?

**3. AFB/EFB Inspection Protocols for Commercial Intake**

Our intake standards include visual AFB/EFB inspection of ≥10% of each incoming load, with immediate quarantine for any suspect colonies. We are designing our staff training protocol and exploring whether MDARD might provide periodic inspection support or training consultation.

*Request:* Would MDARD be interested in a consultation role or annual inspection partnership with our facility? Given that we expect 4,000 hives from ~20–50 Michigan beekeepers each winter, our facility could serve as an efficient single-point node for MDARD's disease surveillance — rather than inspectors visiting dozens of individual apiaries.

**4. APHIS National Honey Bee Survey Data for Michigan**

*Request:* Does MDARD participate in or have access to the USDA APHIS National Honey Bee Survey (NHBS) Michigan-specific data on AFB prevalence, Varroa loads, or colony health by county? If so, can you direct us to the appropriate contact for accessing this data for facility siting and intake risk assessment purposes?

I recognize these are detailed questions, and I am happy to schedule a phone call at your convenience to discuss any or all of them. Our goal is to design a facility that is a genuine partner to MDARD — reducing disease transmission risk across Michigan's commercial hive population by concentrating health inspections at our intake point and sharing records with your office.

Thank you for your work maintaining Michigan's apiary health infrastructure. I look forward to your guidance.
