### About the Facility

Our proposed indoor bee storage would:
- Accept hives in mid-October to mid-November (optimized for the early October intake window that [USDA ARS research](https://www.ars.usda.gov/news-events/news/research-news/2020/helping-honey-bees-make-it-through-winter-with-early-cold-storage/) shows achieves 82% survival vs. 76% for November intake)
- Maintain temperature 38–42°F, humidity 50–65%, CO₂ <1,500 ppm, in complete darkness
- Release colonies in February–March ready for Michigan spring or California almond pollination
- Operate as a Michigan resource — no 2,000-mile trucking to Idaho

### Why Your Input Matters

The winter 2024–25 season saw the **highest commercial colony loss on record nationwide — 40.7%** ([AIA/Auburn 2025](https://apiaryinspectors.org/US-beekeeping-survey-24-25)). Michigan beekeepers entered 2026 facing the consequences of a brutal season. Indoor storage is a proven intervention — but it only works if it's designed around how Michigan beekeepers actually operate.

Your answers will determine:
- Whether our pricing is realistic
- Whether our intake schedule fits your fall management calendar
- Whether our biosecurity requirements are practical or prohibitive
- How we design the logistics for trucks, pallets, and loading zones

We want to build something Michigan beekeepers will actually use. That starts here.
