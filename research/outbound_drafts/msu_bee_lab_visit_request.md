Dear Dr. Milbrath and Dan,

My name is Amira Bloom, and I serve as the Apiary Specialist and Pollination Ecologist for the CEA Campus Design Council, a team developing an integrated controlled-environment agriculture campus in Grand Rapids, Michigan. Our project includes a 4,000-hive climate-controlled indoor winter honey bee storage facility — the first commercial-scale overwintering operation in Michigan — and I am writing to request a site visit to the MSU Pollinator Performance Center and your indoor storage facility.

Your work is, simply, the state reference. I have studied the [MSU indoor honey bee storage article on CANR](https://www.canr.msu.edu/resources/msu-indoor-honey-bee-storage) closely, and the design decisions you made — upgrading wall insulation from R-13 to R-25, selecting the KingTech Refer Unit after HOBO weather data analysis, building in the CO₂ "burp" fan with text alerts — represent exactly the kind of grounded, iterative engineering thinking our 4,000-hive design must honor and extend.

We are working from a very different scale: 100× your current 40-colony research facility, which means our engineering challenges (multi-zone refrigeration, pressurized floor airflow, commercial palletization, biosecurity flow between intake and release) are substantial. But the foundational principles — temperature 38–42°F, darkness, CO₂ management, one-way biosecurity flow — are yours to define for Michigan. I want to learn from you directly, not just from publications.

**What I'd like to learn from a site visit:**

1. **Operational lessons from Fall 2023 and subsequent seasons:** What surprised you? What would you build differently? The USDA ARS funding through Tucson for optimal intake timing — what are the most recent results, and how should commercial operators think about October vs. November intake in Michigan's climate specifically?

2. **Sensor and alert system design:** At 4,000 hives, we are scoping a SCADA-level monitoring system. What sensors have proven most critical? What alert conditions have you triggered, and how quickly were problems resolved?

3. **Colony management protocols during storage:** How often do staff enter the room? What do you look for? Has the CO₂ reader ever exceeded safe thresholds? What is the protocol?

4. **The Pollinator Performance Center's controlled climate room plans:** The CANR website mentions future controlled climate rooms for indoor overwintering research. Is there an opportunity to align our commercial facility design with the PPC's research needs? We would welcome a formal data-sharing partnership — every colony we store contributes to Michigan-specific overwintering survival data that the field urgently needs.

5. **Beekeeper engagement in Michigan:** Who are the commercial operators in Michigan most ready to try indoor storage? Are there existing MBA or extension relationships we should be building on?

I am available for a site visit at your convenience — I can work around your schedule entirely, including field day season. The visit would be 2–4 hours, and I would bring our project's structural engineer and HVAC consultant if that is useful (or come alone for a first conversation).

Thank you for building Michigan's first indoor honey bee storage facility and making it openly documented. The beekeeper community — and the bees — are better for it.
