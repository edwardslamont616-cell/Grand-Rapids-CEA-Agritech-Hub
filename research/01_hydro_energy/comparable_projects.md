# COMPARABLE SMALL HYDRO PROJECTS — MICHIGAN AND MIDWEST
## CEA Campus Design Council / Dr. Lena Watts
### April 2026

This table documents five Michigan/Midwest run-of-river and low-head hydroelectric projects with confirmed capacity factors, permit status, and lessons learned. All are on rivers of similar or comparable scale to the Grand River. Data are drawn from FERC project records, utility documents, and the [Hydropower Reform Coalition eLibrary](https://hydroreform.org).

## PROJECT DETAIL PROFILES

### 1. Ada Dam — Thornapple River, Ada, MI (FERC P-3607)

**Owner:** Eagle Creek Renewable Energy  
**Capacity:** 1,400 kW, single Vertical Kaplan turbine  
**Annual generation:** "More than 5 million kWh" in a typical year  
**Capacity factor:** ~40.8%  
**FERC status:** Exemption from licensing, issued 1982 (pre-HREA; equivalent to current 10-MW exemption pathway)  
**River context:** Thornapple River, a tributary of the Grand River — same watershed as the CEA project

**Lessons learned:**
- Single Kaplan turbine on a regulated agricultural/recreational river achieves ~40–41% CF despite relatively stable baseflows.
- Summer low-flow months (July–September) depress annual CF substantially below theoretical maximum.
- Operational since the early 1980s; exemption pathway avoided full FERC licensing, but fish/wildlife conditions still apply.
- Ada is ~12 miles upstream of the Grand River confluence — directly comparable hydrological setting.

**Source:** [Eagle Creek Renewable Energy — Thornapple River Facilities](https://www.eaglecreekre.com/facilities/operating-facilities/thornapple-river-hydro-facilities); [GridInfo Ada Dam](https://www.gridinfo.com/plant/ada-dam/50433)

### 2. Cascade Dam — Thornapple River, near Grand Rapids, MI (FERC P-6228)

**Owner:** Eagle Creek Renewable Energy  
**Capacity:** 1,600 kW, single Vertical Kaplan turbine  
**Annual generation:** ~7,000 MWh/yr  
**Capacity factor:** ~50.0%  
**FERC status:** Exemption P-6228 (issued 1984); commissioned 1986

**Lessons learned:**
- Higher CF than the upstream Ada project, likely due to greater head availability or more consistent flow regime at this reach.
- Demonstrates that within a single watershed, CF can vary 10+ percentage points based on siting.
- Located within 15 miles of the Grand River — most directly comparable project to the CEA Campus concept.
- At 1,600 kW, Cascade generates 7 GWh/yr — versus a 400 kW unit at the CEA site generating at most 2.1 GWh/yr.
- **Key implication for CEA project:** A 400 kW unit on the Grand River (a much larger river than Thornapple) could reasonably achieve 45–55% CF in a favorable side-channel configuration — consistent with the Cascade benchmark.

**Source:** [Eagle Creek Renewable Energy](https://www.eaglecreekre.com/facilities/operating-facilities/thornapple-river-hydro-facilities)

---

### 3. Croton Dam — Muskegon River, Newaygo County, MI (FERC P-2468)

**Owner:** Consumers Energy (pending sale to Confluence Hydro)  
**Capacity:** 8,850 kW (four generating units)  
**Annual generation:** 36,345.5 MWh/yr  
**Capacity factor:** **46.6%**  
**FERC License:** P-2468, issued July 10, 1994; expires June 26, 2034  
**River context:** Muskegon River — larger than Grand River tributary reaches, similar seasonal hydrology

**Lessons learned:**
- A reservoir dam with four-unit configuration achieves only 46.6% CF — demonstrating that even regulated (storage) hydro rarely exceeds 50% CF on Midwest rivers.
- Consumers Energy is selling all 13 Michigan hydro facilities to Confluence Hydro (Hull Street Energy subsidiary) — [Interlochen Public Radio, September 2025](https://www.interlochenpublicradio.org/2025-09-10/consumers-picks-private-equity-fund-to-buy-michigan-dams-for-1-apiece). Cost of energy from these dams is **nine times** the cost of Consumers' next-most-expensive power source, illustrating the economic challenge of small Michigan hydro.
- FERC relicensing is complex, expensive, and increasingly focused on environmental mitigation (fish passage, minimum flows, aquatic habitat).

**Source:** [Hydropower Reform Coalition — Croton P-2468](https://hydroreform.org/hydro-project/croton-p-2468/)

---

### 4. Tippy Dam — Manistee River, Manistee County, MI

**Owner:** Consumers Energy (pending sale to Confluence Hydro)  
**Capacity:** 21,000 kW  
**Average production:** 7.31 MW (published by Consumers Energy, July 2023)  
**Capacity factor:** **34.8%**  
**FERC License:** Expires ~2034 (Manistee River; license shared with Hodenpyl)

**Lessons learned:**
- Tippy was historically operated in **peaking mode** (high CF during peak hours, stored water during off-peak). In 1989/1994, it was mandated to convert to **run-of-river (ROR) operation** under FERC relicensing agreement — a landmark case studied by Yale's [Kotchen et al.](https://resources.environment.yale.edu/kotchen/pubs/manistee.pdf).
- After converting to ROR, producer costs increased ~$310,000/year as the plant could no longer dispatch to peak prices.
- **The ROR switch reduced effective CF significantly** compared to peaking operation. This is a critical cautionary lesson: ROR operation is environmentally preferred but economically less efficient.
- Tippy's current 34.8% CF on a 21 MW unit illustrates that Michigan hydro rarely achieves above 50% CF under run-of-river operating constraints.
- [Flagged: Kotchen et al. source is pre-2020 (1994 data); operational conditions have continued to evolve.]

**Source:** [Consumers Energy — Tippy Dam Economic Contribution Study, July 2023](https://www.consumersenergy.com/-/media/CE/Documents/company/electric-generation/renewables/hydro-future/tippy-dam/tippy-dam-economic-contribution-report.pdf); [Yale — Benefit-Cost Analysis of Dam Relicensing, Kotchen et al. (flagged: older source)](https://resources.environment.yale.edu/kotchen/pubs/manistee.pdf)

### 5. Hardy Dam — Muskegon River, Newaygo County, MI (FERC License)

**Owner:** Consumers Energy (pending sale to Confluence Hydro)  
**Capacity:** ~32,000 kW  
**FERC License:** Expires 2035; currently under FERC scrutiny for spillway flood control compliance  
**Current status:** Serious regulatory constraints

**Lessons learned:**
- Hardy Dam faces a FERC-required spillway upgrade estimated at $350 million due to updated probable maximum flood (PMF) calculations — [Bridge Michigan, March 24, 2026](https://bridgemi.com/michigan-environment-watch/consumers-seeks-to-delay-millions-in-flood-control-upgrades-as-it-pursues-dam-sales/).
- The spillway deficiency means the dam does not meet current federal flood control standards for high-hazard hydropower dams, illustrating the ongoing regulatory and safety burden of Michigan hydro infrastructure.
- Hardy's situation underscores that **FERC relicensing for any new hydro project in Michigan will involve significantly enhanced environmental and safety conditions** compared to projects licensed in the 1980s–1990s.
- The Hydro Relicensing Coalition has asked FERC to order year-round water drawdown pending spillway resolution — an example of how third-party intervenors can constrain hydro operations.

**Source:** [Bridge Michigan — Hardy Dam Flood Control Delay, March 24, 2026](https://bridgemi.com/michigan-environment-watch/consumers-seeks-to-delay-millions-in-flood-control-upgrades-as-it-pursues-dam-sales/)
