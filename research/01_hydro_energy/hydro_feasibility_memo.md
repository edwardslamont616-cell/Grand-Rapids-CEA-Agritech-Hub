## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The proposed 400 kW run-of-river microturbine on the Grand River is **technically feasible in flow terms alone** — the Grand River carries ample water — but faces **three compounding fatal constraints** that together make the in-channel concept unviable as proposed:

1. **The 83% self-sufficiency claim is physically impossible.** It implies a 100% capacity factor; realistic run-of-river projects on comparable Midwest rivers achieve 35–55% capacity factors. Corrected annual energy output ranges from **1.47–2.10 GWh/yr** (42–60% CF), not 3.504 GWh/yr.

2. **The Lower Grand River Habitat Restoration Project is now funded and entering construction summer 2026.** Four existing low-head dams — which provide essentially all available hydraulic head in the project reach — are being removed. Any in-channel structure dependent on those dams will lose its head source before it could be permitted or built. A new in-channel structure would directly conflict with a $14.56 million City-contract project backed by $11.03 million in NRCS federal funds.

3. **The permitting path is multi-agency, multi-year, and environmentally contentious.** EGLE Part 301/303, USACE Section 404, FERC licensing, ESA Section 7 consultation (federally endangered snuffbox mussel), and USFWS review each add 12–36 months. In this restoration context, denial or indefinite delay is a plausible outcome.

**DFS Recommendation:** Pursue the **PV + BESS hybrid (Phase 1)** immediately to secure energy reliability and investor confidence. Reserve the **side-channel bypass hydro concept** as a Phase 2 research track pending the restoration project's completion and new grade control structure engineering. Abandon the in-channel concept as proposed.

## 1. CONTEXT AND PROBLEM STATEMENT

### 1.1 The Claimed Energy Balance — and Why It Fails

The project narrative claims a 400 kW unit produces 3.504 GWh/year, providing 83% self-sufficiency for a 100,000 SF six-layer CEA facility.

**Check the arithmetic:**

| Parameter | Claimed | Physical Reality |
|---|---|---|
| Installed capacity | 400 kW | 400 kW |
| Annual hours | 8,760 | 8,760 |
| Implied annual energy | 3,504 MWh | 3,504 MWh |
| Implied capacity factor | **100.0%** | **Physically impossible** |
| Realistic CF range (run-of-river, Midwest) | — | **42–60%** |
| Realistic annual energy (42–60% CF) | — | **1,472–2,102 MWh/yr** |

No run-of-river project in the United States achieves 100% capacity factor. Comparable Michigan/Midwest projects (Croton Dam, Ada Dam, Cascade Dam, Tippy Dam) operate at **35–50% CF** (see [comparable_projects.md](comparable_projects.md)). The [U.S. DOE Hydropower Market Report (2021)](https://www.energy.gov/sites/prod/files/2021/01/f82/us-hydropower-market-report-full-2021.pdf) documents average run-of-river capacity factors of **45%** nationally.

### 1.2 Revised Energy Balance

**Facility Annual Energy Demand (100,000 SF, 6-layer CEA):**

Estimated peak electrical demand for a 6-layer 100,000 SF facility with supplemental LED lighting, HVAC, dehumidification, pumps, and controls is approximately 2,400–2,600 kW. At a building load factor of ~75% (lights-off overnight, seasonal HVAC variation), annual demand is estimated at **15–17 GWh/yr**. This is consistent with published benchmarks for equivalent-scale vertical farm facilities.

| Hydro Scenario | CF | Annual Output | Self-Sufficiency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Claimed (as proposed)** | **100%** | **3.504 GWh** | **~22%** (not 83%) |
| Optimistic (side-channel, 6 ft head) | 60% | 2.10 GWh | ~13% |
| Mid-range (in-channel, 5 ft head) | 52% | 1.82 GWh | ~11% |
| Conservative (post-dam removal, 4 ft head) | 42% | 1.47 GWh | ~9% |

**Note:** Even the claimed 3.504 GWh/yr — if achievable — represents only ~22% of estimated facility demand, not 83%. Either the 83% claim is based on a 10× smaller facility model, or the energy demand estimate has been dramatically understated. Both errors require correction before investor or City presentations.

## 2. LOWER GRAND RIVER RESTORATION PROJECT — CONFLICT ANALYSIS

### 2.1 Project Status (March–April 2026)

On March 23, 2026, the [City of Grand Rapids and Grand Rapids Whitewater announced](https://www.grandrapidsmi.gov/city-news/posts/city-of-grand-rapids-and-grand-rapids-whitewater-announce-federal-funding-approval-for-lower-grand-river-habitat-restoration-project/) that USDA NRCS authorized **$11,026,695.43** in Watershed Protection and Flood Prevention Program (PL-566) funding. This represents the **final authorization needed** — all major federal and state permits are now in place.

The [City Commission approved a $14.56 million construction contract with Taplin Group LLC](https://www.michiganpublic.org/transportation-infrastructure/2026-02-25/project-to-remove-dams-in-grand-river-to-begin-this-summer) in February 2026. **In-river construction begins summer 2026.**

### 2.2 What Is Being Removed

The Lower Reach project (I-196 to Fulton Street) removes **four dangerous low-head dams** that currently impound the river in the CEA project reach. These dams are the source of essentially all available hydraulic head for any proposed turbine. They will be replaced with:
- Two channel-wide grade control structures (rock, boulder, alluvium)
- Over 125 habitat boulders
- Three J-hook structures at river edges
- Restored natural rapids and riffle habitat

Per [Grand Rapids Whitewater's project page](https://grandrapidswhitewater.org/the-project/), the design was developed over years of coordination with EGLE and MDNR. The grade control structures are natural features — not impoundment structures — and will provide at most 2–4 feet of hydraulic head, insufficient to reliably power a 400 kW turbine without a supplemental intake/bypass structure.

### 2.3 Upper Reach — Parallel NEPA Process

A separate [USACE EIS for the Upper Reach Revitalization](https://www.lrd.usace.army.mil/Missions/Planning/Display/Article/4443747/grand-river-upper-reach-revitalization-sea-lamprey-control-and-habitat-improvem/) (6th Street Dam removal and sea lamprey barrier) is underway. Public scoping ran April–May 2026. Draft EIS expected Spring–Summer 2027; Final EIS Winter 2028. Any hydro structure in or near the 6th Street Dam reach would be pulled into this EIS process.

**Key constraint:** The USACE EIS explicitly concerns construction in the reach from Ann Street to Bridge Street — immediately upstream of the CEA project site.

## 3. FERC JURISDICTIONAL ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Is a FERC License Required?

A hydroelectric project on the Grand River in Grand Rapids requires FERC jurisdiction analysis under the Federal Power Act. The Grand River is a navigable water of the United States; any new structure in its channel triggers the FERC licensing requirement (18 CFR § 4.30).

**Conduit Exemption (18 CFR § 4.90):** Under [Section 30(b) of the Federal Power Act, implemented at 18 CFR § 4.90–4.96](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/18/4.90), a conduit exemption applies to facilities using a **man-made conduit** operated primarily for **non-hydroelectric purposes** (agricultural, municipal, or industrial water supply). The Grand River is a natural waterway, not a man-made conduit. **The conduit exemption does not apply.**

**10-MW Exemption:** Available for projects ≤10 MW at an existing dam or natural water feature. This pathway requires NEPA analysis, three-stage pre-filing consultation, and mandatory fish/wildlife conditions under Section 30(c). With the restoration project actively removing structures in this reach and an endangered mussel population, the 10-MW exemption process would face mandatory Section 7 ESA consultation and almost certainly require an Environmental Assessment or EIS.

**Full License:** Projects on navigable waters require a full FERC license (up to 50-year term). Timeline from application to license: **typically 5–7 years** for contested sites.

**Bottom line:** There is no regulatory shortcut. Either a 10-MW exemption (2–4 years, best case) or a full license (5–7 years) is required. In the context of an active restoration EIS involving the same reach, the timeline extends further.

## 4. GRAND RIVER FLOW ANALYSIS

*Full analysis in [usgs_flow_analysis.md](usgs_flow_analysis.md)*

**USGS Gage 04119000 (Grand River at Grand Rapids)** — drainage area 4,900 sq mi; record from 1901.

| Statistic | Flow (cfs) |
|---|---|
| Historical minimum (1963) | 1,000 |
| 25th percentile | 1,980 |
| **Median** | **3,020** |
| **Mean** | **4,110** |
| 75th percentile | 5,390 |
| Historical maximum (1974) | 15,900 |

The Grand River is a large, perennial river with high baseflows relative to a 400 kW design requirement. A 5 ft head unit would require a design flow of approximately 950 cfs — well below the 25th percentile — suggesting theoretical flow exceedance of 85%+ of the time. However:

- **Post-dam removal, available head is 2–5 ft** (grade control structures only), not 8–12 ft as would be possible with dams in place.
- **Ice shutdowns** (typically December–February) reduce operational hours.
- **Flood protection protocols** during high-flow events may require shutdown above certain thresholds.
- **Maintenance downtime** typically 2–4 weeks/year for turbines of this class.

Accounting for these factors, **realistic CF = 42–55%** for a properly engineered post-restoration hydro system.

**Seasonal pattern:** MSU Extension and USGS data confirm spring peaks (March median ~7,000 cfs) and summer lows (July–October median below 2,000 cfs). Summer lows can approach or fall below design flow for a low-head system, further reducing CF.

## 5. BRANCHING OPTIONS — TREE-OF-THOUGHT ANALYSIS

### Option A: In-Channel Run-of-River (As Proposed)

**Technology:** Ossberger crossflow or Kaplan axial-flow turbine, in-channel installation.

**Fatal constraints:**
- Loses head source as soon as Lower Reach dams are removed (summer 2026).
- Conflicts directly with a fully-funded, permitted restoration project.
- No available site for a new impoundment structure in this reach.
- FERC license + EGLE + USACE + ESA = 5–7+ year timeline.
- Federally endangered snuffbox mussel population recently relocated — any disturbance triggers ESA Section 7.

**Verdict:** Not viable. Abandon.

### Option B: Side-Channel Bypass (Best Hydro Option)

**Technology:** Small intake structure at upstream grade control structure, bypass channel to turbine house, discharge downstream of next grade control structure. Similar to [Ada Dam configuration](https://www.eaglecreekre.com/facilities/operating-facilities/thornapple-river-hydro-facilities) on Thornapple River.

**Advantages:**
- Does not impede natural flow regime (no head impoundment in main channel).
- Reduced conflict with restoration project objectives.
- FERC 10-MW exemption pathway viable (smaller footprint, less environmental impact).
- Better flow control and operational flexibility.

**Constraints:**
- Requires coordination agreement with City of Grand Rapids and Grand Rapids Whitewater (they control the grade control structure design).
- Still requires EGLE Part 301, USACE Section 404, FERC 10-MW exemption.
- Available head from grade control structures: 3–5 ft maximum.
- At 5 ft head, 82% efficiency: design flow ~950 cfs; annual energy at 52% CF = **1.82 GWh/yr**.
- Site acquisition/easement at Monroe Ave NE and river edge.

**Timeline:** 24–36 months minimum (assuming coordination, no EIS required).

**Verdict:** Viable as Phase 2 research track — but not before 2028–2029 earliest.

### Option C: Rooftop PV + BESS Hybrid (Recommended Phase 1)

**Technology:** Ground-mount and/or rooftop PV array (target: 500–750 kW) + lithium iron phosphate BESS (1,000–2,000 kWh usable capacity) + Consumers Energy grid interconnection as backup.

**Advantages:**
- Deployable 2026–2027 without river permits.
- No FERC, USACE, or EGLE water permits required.
- Qualifies for **30% federal ITC** (Investment Tax Credit, 26 USC § 48) through 2032.
- **USDA REAP grant**: up to 50% of eligible project costs (IRA-funded REAP, max $1M grant) for a CEA facility classified as agricultural producer. Note: Grand Rapids is urban (population >50,000); confirm rural area eligibility with USDA Rural Development. If urban, REAP may not apply — confirm before relying on this.
- Michigan commercial solar: ~$1.83–$3.00/W installed (2025–2026); after 30% ITC, effective cost $1.28–$2.10/W.
- 500 kW system cost: ~$915,000–$1,500,000 before ITC; **$641,000–$1,050,000 after ITC**.
- Michigan Alternative Energy Property Tax Exemption: installed PV system does not increase assessed property value.
- Michigan Renewable Energy Credits (MRECs) via MIRECS system: additional revenue stream.

**Energy contribution:**
- Michigan average PV capacity factor: ~14–16% (EnergySage, [April 2026](https://www.energysage.com/local-data/solar-panel-cost/mi/)).
- 500 kW array: 500 kW × 0.15 CF × 8,760 hr = **657 MWh/yr** (~4.1% of facility demand).
- 750 kW array: 750 kW × 0.15 CF × 8,760 hr = **985 MWh/yr** (~6.2% of facility demand).
- BESS (2,000 kWh) enables peak shaving, demand charge reduction (significant for commercial accounts).

**Verdict:** **Strongly recommended as Phase 1 anchor.** Addresses investor energy resilience concerns immediately, generates ITC and potential REAP credits, and does not preclude Phase 2 hydro development.

### Option D: Hybrid (PV + BESS + Future Side-Channel Hydro)

The optimal long-term configuration combines:
- Phase 1 (2026–2027): 500–750 kW PV + 1,500–2,000 kWh BESS
- Phase 2 (2028–2030): Side-channel bypass hydro (est. 200–400 kW) pending restoration project completion and grade control structure engineering
- Phase 3 (ongoing): Consumers Energy net billing for export credits

**Projected combined self-sufficiency (Phase 1+2):**
- PV (750 kW, 15% CF): ~985 MWh/yr
- Hydro (400 kW, 50% CF): ~1,752 MWh/yr
- Total: ~2,737 MWh/yr
- Self-sufficiency: ~17% of 16 GWh/yr facility demand

This is realistic. It is far short of 83%, but it is buildable, fundable, and environmentally defensible.

## 6. DFS RECOMMENDATION WITH RATIONALE

**Applying Depth-First Search: pursue the most promising, actionable path fully before evaluating alternatives.**

**Step 1 (Immediate — 2026 Q3):** Commission a formal energy audit of the proposed CEA facility. Quantify actual peak demand and annual consumption before any further energy system investment. The 83% self-sufficiency claim cannot be evaluated without this baseline.

**Step 2 (2026 Q3–Q4):** Engage Consumers Energy Level 3 DER interconnection process (400 kW hydro or PV system falls within Level 3: 150–550 kW). File Pre-Application Report. Begin site assessment for rooftop/ground-mount PV.

**Step 3 (2026 Q4–2027 Q1):** File PV + BESS system design, ITC applications, and REAP pre-screening (confirm eligibility). Target commissioning within 12 months.

**Step 4 (2027):** Attend USACE Upper Reach EIS public process as stakeholder. Request coordination meeting with Grand Rapids Whitewater regarding post-restoration grade control structure design and potential side-channel bypass feasibility. This positions the project for Phase 2 without prematurely committing capital.

**Step 5 (2028–2029):** If coordination with GRWW and City yields favorable grade control structure engineering, initiate FERC 10-MW exemption pre-filing consultation (three-stage process per 18 CFR § 4.38). Simultaneously file EGLE Part 301 pre-application meeting request (see [egle_preapp_request.md](../outbound_drafts/egle_preapp_request.md)).

**Do not proceed with the in-channel concept as proposed.** It cannot survive the permitting timeline, conflicts with a fully-funded federal restoration project, and is based on a physically impossible energy claim. Investor presentations that include the 83% figure should be corrected immediately to avoid securities liability.

## 7. UNCERTAINTY AND HONEST LIMITATIONS

- **Facility energy demand:** The 16 GWh/yr estimate for a 6-layer 100,000 SF CEA is a reasonable order-of-magnitude, but CEA energy demand varies widely based on crop type, photoperiod, HVAC strategy, and lighting technology. A formal energy model is required.
- **Post-restoration hydraulic head:** Until the grade control structure designs are finalized by Taplin Group and approved by EGLE/USACE, available head for a bypass hydro system cannot be calculated with confidence.
- **REAP eligibility for urban CEA:** Grand Rapids has a population well above 50,000. REAP rural area eligibility must be confirmed with USDA Rural Development before this funding source is relied upon.
- **ITC status under current Administration:** The 30% ITC is currently legislated through 2032 under the Inflation Reduction Act, but ongoing regulatory uncertainty means this figure should be confirmed at time of application.
- **USGS flow data:** The statistics cited are from the historical record at USGS 04119000. Post-restoration hydrodynamics in the Lower Reach will change as dam-induced backwater effects are removed; actual design-point head and flow conditions will require site-specific hydraulic modeling.
