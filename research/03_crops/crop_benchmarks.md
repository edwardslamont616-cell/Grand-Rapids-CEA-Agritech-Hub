## Executive Summary

This document compiles peer-reviewed and extension-sourced yield, cycle, pricing, and margin benchmarks for all five crop categories in the Grand Rapids Hub pro forma. Each table includes honest ranges, notes on variability drivers, and inline citations. Numbers here form the evidence base for the 52-week production calendar and investor revenue model.

**Council context:** The original pro forma projects $14.6M Year 1 CEA+processing revenue. The benchmarks below support a realistic $7–10M Y1 with full ramp by Y3.

> **Key benchmark:** OSU Kroggel cites greenhouse benchmark yield of 50–60 kg/m²/yr, with a record of 100 kg/m²/yr (Village Farms). The [PubMed 2023 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37514238/) found that across 94% of studies, CEA lettuce averages **3.68 kg/m²** per crop vs. 1.88 kg/m² field-grown, with vertical stacked systems pushing to 6.88 kg/m².

> **Reject rates:** Industry standard 5–12% for leafy greens; tipburn on lettuce is leading culprit (linked to calcium deficiency + inadequate airflow at growing tips). Target ≤8% reject for a well-managed facility.

### Gross Margin Estimate (Single-Tier Greenhouse)

At 30 kg/m²/yr average yield (lettuce), $5.50/kg wholesale:
- **Revenue/m²/yr:** ~$165
- **Estimated COGS/m²/yr (energy, labor, nutrients, packaging):** ~$100–$120
- **Gross margin:** ~27–40%
- Note: Vertical farms with higher energy costs may compress to 15–25% gross margin at the farm level.

**Key data points:**
- UFL study: Vertical tower herb yields of **215–697 oz/tower** (32 plants/tower). Highest yielding: arugula (696 oz), basil (585 oz), parsley (437 oz) per season. ([UFL Greenhouse Herb Production](https://svaec.ifas.ufl.edu/media/svaecifasufledu/docs/pdf/svreports/crops/leafygreens/Fresh-cut-herbs-in-Vertical-hydroponic-Systems.pdf))
- Basil: increasing plant density to 4-inch centers under high DLI significantly increases yield per square foot ([Greenhouse Grower](https://www.greenhousegrower.com/production/increase-planting-densities-to-increase-hydroponic-basil-yields/)). Optimal PPFD 200–400 µmol/m²/s (above 400 decreases flavor—consumer preference peaks at 200–300 µmol) ([MSU Thesis](https://d.lib.msu.edu/etd/48847)).
- pH: Optimal 5.5 for basil, dill, parsley; 6.0 for sage and mint ([Iowa State University study](https://dr.lib.iastate.edu/bitstreams/7507538d-0ebb-42af-bb10-72d8c72859bb/download)).

**Commercial example:**
- Eden Green (Cleburne, TX): grows top 10 herb varieties in vertical greenhouse under patent-pending mobile light system, white-labeled for Robinson Fresh and GFS retail clients ([FoodNavigator-USA, 2024](https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2024/04/03/eden-green-s-herb-program-provides-a-cost-effective-solution-to-international-imports/)).

## 4. Saffron (Crocus sativus) in CEA

### Background

Saffron is the world's most valuable spice by weight—retail prices of **$8,500–$12,000/kg** for premium Sargol grade. However, it is a **triploid sterile plant** propagated exclusively via corms, requires 2–3 seasons to peak yield, and harvesting (stigma picking) remains **entirely manual**. These factors make it a poor Year 1 revenue driver.

### Maturation Timeline

| Season | Status | Expected Yield (outdoor field baseline) | Notes |
|--------|--------|----------------------------------------|-------|
| Year 1 | Establishment | Near-zero / loss — corms focus on vegetative growth | Corm acquisition ~$10–$20/corm at food-grade 8g+ weight |
| Year 2 | Partial yield | 30–50% of mature yield | Daughter corms multiply; yield builds |
| Year 3–4 | Peak yield | Max productivity; begin new rotation | [Scientific Reports Saffron Yield Study, 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12819505/) |
| Year 5–6 | Decline | Yield drops 20–91% | Replanting necessary |

### Stigma Yield per m²

| Environment | Corm Density | Flowers/m²/yr | Dry Stigma Yield | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|--------------|-----------------|-------|
| Outdoor field | 100–200 corms/m² | 100–200 | 1.0–3.0 g/m²/yr | Field baseline |
| High tunnel/greenhouse (University of Vermont) | Optimal | — | **1.39 g/m²** (Y1) | $15/g projected gross = **$4,480** in UVM high tunnel ([UVM Saffron Workshop 2018](https://www.uvm.edu/~saffron/Old/Workshops/Saffron%20Workshop%202018/Saffron%20From%20Production%20to%20Processing%20-%20Arash%20G.pdf)) |
| Optimized CEA aeroponic | 150 corms/m², 4 harvest cycles | 600 flowers/m²/yr | **~4.0 g/m²/yr** | Theoretical; [Agritecture CEA Saffron Analysis, 2022](https://www.agritecture.com/blog/2022/11/10/saffron-should-we-grow-the-most-expensive-spice-in-the-world-using-cea) |
| CEA research prototype (Azafarm, Spain 2025) | Controlled | 3× field yield | 4.0–6.0 g/m²/yr est. | Prototype stage; [Hortidaily, 2025](https://www.hortidaily.com/article/9792143/saffron-cultivation-advances-indoors-we-already-produce-2-5-to-3-times-more-than-in-the-field/) |
| Greenhouse (Saudi Arabia, Frontiers 2025) | 100 corms/m², 8 cm depth | 337–553 flowers/m² | 3.2–5.2 g/m² | [Frontiers in Plant Science, 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12235264/) |

### Break-Even Analysis

| Scenario | Scale (m²) | CAPEX (corms + infra) | Annual COGS | Peak Yield (Y3–4) | Revenue at $15/g | Break-Even |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|------------|
| Research/education (small) | 50 m² | ~$25,000–$40,000 | ~$15,000/yr | ~200 g | ~$3,000 | Never as revenue driver; justified as R&D/tourism asset |
| Commercial (ambitious) | 500 m² | ~$200,000+ | ~$80,000/yr | ~2,000 g | ~$30,000 | 3–4 year negative cash flow minimum |

> **Council conclusion:** Saffron in Y1 generates **negative revenue contribution**. Its value lies in research differentiation, agri-tourism tours ("harvest the world's most expensive spice"), and educational programming. See the *saffron_reframing_memo.md* for repositioning strategy.

### Yield, Cycle, Pollination

| Parameter | Benchmark | Notes | Source |

| Yield, greenhouse benchmark | **50–60 kg/m²/yr** | Various operators, multi-span glasshouse | [OSU Kroggel CEA PDF](https://mastergardener.osu.edu/sites/mgv/files/imce/Hydroponics%20and%20CEA_Kroggel.pdf) |

| Cycle (indeterminate) | 9–11 months/crop | Year-round production with overlap | Standard CEA practice |
| EC setpoints | 3.0–5.0 dS/m | Higher EC = firmer fruit, lower yield | Wageningen UR practice |
| pH | 5.8–6.3 | Widen slightly at fruiting | Standard |
| Temperature | 20–24°C day / 16–18°C night | Tomato-specific; critical for fruit set | Standard |

### Pollination: Bumblebees vs. Honey Bees

| Pollinator | Effectiveness | Key Data | Verdict |
|-----------|--------------|----------|---------|
| **Bumblebee** (*B. impatiens*) | **Industry standard** | 57.7% higher fruit yield vs. no pollination; heavier fruit (218.6g vs. 191.5g vs. PGR); higher sugar content; [Frontiers in Plant Science, 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12006744/) | **Preferred for greenhouse tomato** |
| Honey bee (*A. mellifera*) | Acceptable substitute | Comparable sugar/pH effects but smaller fruit; useful where bumblebees unavailable | Secondary option |
| Plant growth regulator (PGR/vibrator) | Labor-intensive | Lower fruit weight and quality; chemical residues possible | Avoid in premium CEA context |

**Bumblebee stocking rate:** 7–15 colonies/hectare ([OSU Bumblebee Pollination Factsheet, 2023](https://ohioline.osu.edu/factsheet/ent-0092)).

> **Campus integration:** The Grand Rapids Hub's on-site apiary (Amira Bloom) uses *Apis mellifera* colonies. For the greenhouse tomato zone, procurement of *B. impatiens* commercial colonies (Koppert Natupol, Biobest Tripol) is the standard of care. Honey bee hives can supplement but should not replace bumblebees for tomato.
